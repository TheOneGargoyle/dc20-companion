Creating exciting, fair, and memorable battles is at the heart
of DC20. This document provides tools and guidelines for
building monsters and designing combat encounters that
challenge your players without overwhelming them. You’ll
learn how to balance enemy strength, damage output,
action economy, encounter difficulty, and how many
enemies overall.
DC20 Combat Philosophy
These are the core design philosophies for a standard
DC20 Combat:
- Accuracy: Attacks have a 65% chance to hit targets.
- Balance: A Monster is about as strong as the same level
PC
- Quantity: There are the same amount of monsters
fighting the same number of PCs.
- Difficulty: Combat Encounters are designed  for Medium
difficulty.
- Duration: Combat Encounters last an average of 3
Rounds.
DC Tip: Hard difficulty monsters are designed to be killed by
PCs of the same level in 3 Rounds to ensure the design goal of
3 Round Combats is met.
Example: If an average Combat Encounter lasts 3 Rounds,
a PC makes 1-2 Attacks per Round, and the PC have a 65%
chance to hit the monster with an Attack, then the PC would
make 4-6 Attacks across the 3 Rounds and hit the monster
with 3-4 Attacks. If the monster needs to be defeated in those
3 Rounds, then the Attacks each need to deal 25%-33% of the
monster’s max HP to defeat it.

statistics to strike the right balance between challenge
and fun. By adjusting these numbers, GMs can design
encounters that stay exciting and fair, keeping players
engaged without making the fight too easy or too punishing.

Monster Offense
A monster’s offensive power defines its role in combat and
shapes how players experience the encounter. Whether it’s
a brutal melee bruiser, a cunning ambusher, or an arcane
threat that controls the battlefield, its damage output and
attack design determine how dangerous it feels. Balancing a
monster’s offense ensures that every encounter is tense and
exciting without becoming unfair or overwhelming. The
following guidelines will help you set appropriate damage
values, design balanced attacks, and fine-tune how your
monsters challenge the party.

Monster Attacks
When designing a monster’s Attacks, consider how many
creatures each Attack can target, how much damage it
deals, and how many Action Points (AP) it should cost.
These guidelines help ensure that monsters feel balanced,
dynamic, and appropriate for the difficulty.
Single-Target Attacks: An Attack that targets only 1
creature should cost 1 AP.
Multi-Target Attacks: An Attack that targets 2 or more
creatures (such as an Area Attack) should cost 2 AP. You
can adjust this based on the size and reach of the Area of
Effect.
Example: A typical Level 1 PC has about 9 HP, so a Level 1
monster in a Hard difficulty Combat Encounter should deal
about 2 Damage per Round.
- Single Target Attacks: If the monster makes 2
Attacks per Round, each Attack should cost 1 AP and
deal 1 damage .
- Multiple Target Attacks: If the monster makes 1 Attack
that targets 2 creatures (such as a 3 Space Sphere), the
Attack should cost 2 AP and deal 1 damage. If that
Attack targets 3 or more creatures (such as a 5 Space
Sphere) then it should deal half as much damage (0.5
damage) or cost more AP (such as 3 or 4 AP).
Monster Survivability
A monster’s survivability determines how long it can
stay active in combat and how much effort the party
must invest to defeat it. In DC20, survivability is shaped
primarily by three core stats: Health Points, Defenses,
and Resistances. Balancing these elements ensures your
monsters feel threatening without dragging out the fight
or making the encounter frustrating. Each stat plays a
distinct role in defining how survivable a creature is on the
battlefield.
Health Points (HP)
Health represents a monster’s raw endurance. The more
HP it has, the longer it can withstand attacks before being
defeated. Increasing HP makes a monster more reliable over
time, since it can continue to take hits even when facing
multiple attackers or high-damage characters. However,
giving a monster too much Health can make battles feel
slow or repetitive, so it is often better to pair moderate
HP with other defensive tools like Resistance or crowdcontrol abilities.

Defenses (PD and AD)
A monster’s Precision Defense (PD) and Area Defense
(AD) determine how hard it is to hit.
- Precision Defense (PD): Protects against attacks that
are made against a specific target.
- Area Defense (AD): Protects against wide-reaching
attacks that cover an area, usually containing multiple
targets.
Raising these Defenses reduces how often the monster gets
hit by attacks rather than how much damage it takes. High
Defenses work well for well protected or highly evasive
creatures, but too much avoidance can make encounters
feel unfair or slow if the party struggles to land hits.
Resistances
Resistances reduce the amount of damage a monster takes
after being hit. They represent natural toughness, armor, or
magical wards that blunt the impact of attacks. Granting
Resistance is an effective way to make a monster feel
resilient without relying solely on high HP. For example,
an armored knight might resist physical damage, while an
elemental spirit resists fire or cold.
DC Tip: True damage can’t be reduced or mitigated in any
way, so Resistance doesn’t work against it. It’s recommended
to avoid giving monsters abilities that deal True damage.
Damage Reduction (DR)
Damage Reduction is a special form of Resistance that
only reduces damage from normal Hits. It doesn’t protect
against Heavy Hits or Critical Hits, which bypass this
protection. Against normal Hits, DR functions the same
as Resistance, reducing the monster’s incoming damage by
a set amount or percentage. This makes DR a useful tool
for monsters that should shrug off light attacks but remain
vulnerable to strong, well-placed strikes.
DC Tip: Physical Damage Reduction (PDR) works against
Bludgeoning, Piercing, and Slashing damage. Elemental
Damage Reduction (EDR) works against Cold, Corrosion, Fire,
Lightning, and Poison damage. Mystical Damage Reduction
(MDR) works against Psychic, Radiant, and Umbral damage.
Monster Speed
A monster’s Speed determines how far it can move each
time it takes the Move Action (1 AP). In DC20, most
monsters are designed with a Speed of 5, which matches the
default Speed of PCs. This makes encounters predictable
and balanced, allowing both players and GMs to plan
movement and positioning around similar rates of motion.
Adjusting Speed
Speed should vary for monsters that are naturally faster
or slower than humanoid characters. Creatures known for
agility, flight, or swiftness might have higher Speed to reflect
their mobility, while slower, lumbering monsters should
have lower Speed to emphasize their cumbersome nature.
Fast Monsters: These monsters can harass ranged or
weaker characters, reposition frequently, or threaten
multiple areas of the battlefield. A warhorse might have a
Speed of 8, allowing it to charge across the battlefield and
engage or disengage quickly.
Slow Monsters: These monsters often rely on durability,
area control, or powerful attacks to compensate for their
limited mobility. A turtle or golem might have a Speed of
2, moving deliberately and requiring strategic positioning
to become a threat.
Monster Action Points
Most monsters have 4 Action Points (AP). They use
AP during their Turn to take Actions, or during another
creature’s Turn to take Reactions if the situation allows.
Spending Action Points
How a monster spends its Action Points defines its combat
style, level of threat, and overall impact on the battlefield.
A creature that focuses all its AP on a flurry of attacks can
quickly overwhelm a wounded PC, while one that mixes
movement and strikes can control the battlefield and
pressure multiple targets at once.
- Heavy Attack: Empower a single, devastating attack
(Attack Action + Enhancements).
- Multi-Attack: Make multiple attacks in a single turn
(repeated Attack Action).
- Move & Attack: Move and strike to gain positional
advantage (Move Action + Attack Action).
- Signature Ability: Trigger special abilities, defenses, or
support effects (Spell Action or Feature).
Thoughtful AP use raises the tension of combat and pushes
players to adapt their tactics, manage their resources
wisely, and coordinate as a team to overcome the monster’s
shifting strategies.
Monster Traits
Monsters can be granted Traits to give them unique
strengths, weaknesses, and special abilities. These Traits
help define a monster’s identity, making encounters distinct,
memorable, and strategically engaging. By assigning at
least one Trait per monster, you create opportunities
for players to adapt their strategies and face a variety of
challenges. Traits can highlight a monster’s role in combat,
its personality, or its connection to the environment,
ensuring that every encounter feels different.
Example: A shadowy creature might have a Trait that makes
it difficult to detect but vulnerable to light-based attacks
(such as the Light Spell or Radiant damage).
DC Tip: Certain Traits can increase or decrease a monster’s
damage, HP, or Defenses. It is highly recommended that you
not grant a monster more than 2 of any kind of these Traits.

Monster Trait Value
When referencing the Monster Statistics Table to
determine the appropriate Trait value for a monster at a
given level. The number shown represents the total value
provided by that monster’s Traits. Each Trait a monster
possesses (whether offensive, defensive, or utility-based)
contributes to this total. A single strong Trait might account
for the full value, while several minor Traits could combine
to reach it.
Determining Trait Value
The value is based on how much bonus AP it provides per 3
Round Combat (1 Trait Point = 2 AP).
DC Tip: 1 Monster Trait Point is equal to 1 Ancestry Trait
Point, so you could easily add Ancestry Traits to Monsters!
Granting Class Features
Monsters can also gain Class Features or versions of
them, giving them traits typically associated with PCs. For
instance, a monster could have a Champion’s Adaptive
Tactics, a Cleric’s Channel Divinity, or a Rogue’s Cheap
Shot, allowing for more dynamic and versatile encounters.
A Class Feature is worth roughly 2 Trait value for a monster.
Example: A Goblin with a Rogue’s Cunning Action and
Cheap Shot can strike quickly and exploit vulnerabilities
in the party’s defenses (4 total Trait value). A Giant with a
Barbarian’s Rage Feature can deliver devastating attacks that
test the party’s endurance and planning (2 total Trait value).
DC Tip: Using Class Features on monsters is a great way to
showcase abilities from Classes that are not represented
by the party’s PCs, giving players a sense of the wider
possibilities within the game.

Monster Roles
Monster roles describe how a creature can be used most
effectively in combat. They help GMs select monsters that
work well together and fulfill different functions during a
battle. These roles do not provide mechanical bonuses or
penalties by themselves, but they give insight into how a
monster was designed and how it might behave in combat
alongside others.

Building an Encounter
Designing a well-balanced encounter is one of the most
rewarding parts of running DC20. A good encounter
should challenge the party without overwhelming them,
encourage tactical decision-making, and keep the tension
high. This system uses a level-based approach that closely
aligns monster levels with the levels of the player characters
(PCs), helping Game Masters (GMs) create fair and
exciting battles.
Encounter Budget
When creating a Combat Encounter, the total number of
monster levels should generally match the total number
of PC levels in the party. This creates a balanced, Medium
difficulty Combat Encounter where both sides are near
evenly matched but the party is favored to win. This
guideline ensures the party faces a fair challenge without
excessive risk of a total party defeat, unless luck or strategy
swings heavily in one direction.
Example: A party of 4 x Level 3 PCs would have an Encounter
Budget of 12 (4 x 3 = 12).
Encounter Difficulty
Once you know your party’s total level, compare it to the
combined level of the monsters to determine the difficulty
of the encounter. You can increase or decrease difficulty
of a Combat Encounter by adjusting the damage dealt by
the monsters (see the Monster Damage section) or by
increasing or decreasing their level or quantity as show in
the table below.

Degrees of Difficulty
Deadly: These Combat Encounters are designed to be truly
life-threatening. A frontal assault is rarely wise; survival
often depends on retreat, clever tactics, or exploiting a
narrow advantage.
Very Hard: Victory demands sharp tactics, coordinated
teamwork, and careful positioning. One mistake can shift
the fight, and players must actively work together to turn
the odds.
Hard: Clever parties can seize victory through smart play,
resource management, teamwork, coordination, and sharp
tactics.
Medium: The party holds a modest advantage. The threat
is real enough to keep players alert, but they are expected to
prevail unless they take major risks.
Easy: These encounters should feel safe while remaining
interesting. The party can showcase their abilities without
much danger, making them good warmups or moments of
momentum.
Monster Level Conversion
The simplest way to balance an Encounter is by ensuring
there are an equal number of Monsters and PCs that are of
the same level. However, you can convert a Monster into
multiple Monsters of lower levels, provided their total levels
add up to the same value.
Example: You could convert a Level 6 Monster into 2 x Level
3 Monsters or 3 x Level 2 Monsters.
DC Tip: Monster level conversion doesn’t work as well with
Monsters that are Level 3 or lower. This is because a Level 2
Monster is only ~60% as strong as 2 x Level 1 Monsters (when
you add their HP and damage together), so when you convert
a Level 2 Monster into 2 x Level 1 Monsters you increase the
danger by +75% (the danger should ideally remain the same).
When using Monster level conversion on Level 2 Monsters,
budget the Encounter for 1 Difficulty lower than normal.
For example, you could convert 1 x Level 2 Monster of Hard
difficulty to 2 x Level 1 Monsters of Medium difficulty.

Action Economy
The action economy is one of the key elements that
determines the difficulty of a combat encounter. It
represents the total number of Actions a side can take in a
single Round, measured by their combined Action Points
(AP) The relationship between the monsters’ total AP and
the party’s total AP dictates how frequently each side can
act, attack, reposition, and react to changing conditions on
the battlefield.
Adjusting the Action Economy
If you want to increase or decrease the number of monsters
without changing the encounter’s difficulty, adjust their
available AP so that the total monster AP remains roughly
equal to the total PC AP. This ensures both sides have
comparable opportunities to act each Round, keeping the
flow of combat dynamic and fair.
Advanced Actions
Epic and Legendary monsters gain special options
that expand their tactical reach: Round Actions and
Reaction Points.
Beta Note: Round Actions were formerly called Epic Actions,
and Reaction Points were formerly called Legendary Action
Points (LAP). They were both renamed to distinguish them
from Epic and Legendary monsters respectively, and to make
it clearer what function they have in the game.
Round Actions
Round Actions make large battles feel epic and ensure
powerful creatures maintain presence and pressure
throughout a fight. A monster with Round Actions can
automatically perform the stated Action at any time during
the stated Round, unless the monster is Incapacitated.
These Actions may be the same each Round or unique
to each Round, depending on design intent. Each Round
Action should be roughly equivalent to a 2 AP Action the
monster could take normally.
Reaction Points
Reaction Points allow a monster to act more frequently
outside of its own Turn, keeping the fight dynamic and
unpredictable.
Using Reaction Points
A monster can spend Reaction Points to take Actions,
Reactions, or perform AP Enhancements in the
following ways:
Reactions: The monster can spend Reaction Points to take
Reactions available to them.
Example: If the monster has the ability to perform a Spell
Duel, they could spend 2 AP to challenge a Spell cast by
another creature. Alternatively, they could spend 2 Reaction
Points instead of 2 Action Points to perform the Spell Duel.
Actions (End of PC Turn): At the end of a PC Turn, the
monster can spend Reaction Points instead of Action Points
to take an Action. If an effect prevents you from taking
Reactions, you can still spend Reaction Points to take
Actions at the end of a PC Turn. If multiple PCs share the
same PC Turn, the monster can take a number of Actions
up to the number of PCs that occupy that PC Turn.
Example: If 2 PCs are sharing a Turn (see Shared Initiative
in the Core Rules), when their Turn ends, a monster with
Reaction Points can take 2 Actions (1 Action per PC). They
could take the Attack Action twice, take the Move Action then
the Attack Action, or another other 2 Actions of their choice.
AP Enhancements: When the monster spends Reaction
Points to take an Action or Reaction, they can spend
additional Reaction Points to perform AP Enhancements
with that Action or Reaction. The total amount of Action
Points and Reactions Points spent on an Action or Reaction
can’t exceed 4.
Example: A PC just ended their Turn. The monster can
spend 1 Reaction Point to take the Attack Action and make
an Attack against a target within range. The monster can add
any AP Enhancements to the Attack, spending an amount
of Reaction Points equal to the AP cost required. The AP
cost of the Attack plus the Enhancements can’t exceed 4
AP, because the monster can’t spend more than 4 Reaction
Points per Action.
Regaining Reaction Points
The monster regains all spent Reaction Points at the end
of its Turn.

Animated Armor
Description: These mobile suits of armor are usually
completely hollow on the inside, animated by magic that
gives them a semblance of life. The types of armor they’re
made from vary widely, but a sturdy plate mail is the most
popular and most effective.
Tactics: They control the battlefield with attacks that push
targets into each other (Mace with Bash Enhancement),
and then running through lines of enemies (Pathcarver).
If they have a humanoid ally, they’ll Equip themselves
to their ally to give them extra defense. When an ally is
targeted, they Protect them every chance they get.
Lore: An Animated Armor makes for a great guard for a
location that someone wants to keep undisturbed. They’re
usually used by mages who make them themselves or by
those who are wealthy enough to have someone make the
construct for them. They’re kept as resolute watchers who
can guard an area at all times, attacking anyone who should
pass that doesn’t have clearance by their master.

Bandit
Description: Bandits are as diverse as the many cultures
they come from, but all are well armed and battle worn.
They frequently have scars and tattoos, and speak in gruff,
terse tones.
Tactics: Focus fire on the weakest targets in range first.
Will Reckless Whirlwind if Flanked, and will flee if heavily
injured or outnumbered 2 to 1.
Lore: Bandits are hardened criminals, abandoning
civilization to prey on innocent travelers. They frequently
set up a base of operations, then ambush travelers on nearby
roads.
Bandit Captain
Description: Bandit Captains are as diverse as the many
cultures they come from, but all well armed and battleworn.
They frequently have more scars and tattoos than their
fellow bandits, and are usually larger with commanding
tones.
Tactics: Uses Rally and Help to support allies, then flank
an enemy to attack. Uses Acid Vial if it can hit at least 2
enemies without hitting allies.
Lore: Bandit Captains lead a group of hardened criminals,
abandoning civilization to prey on innocent travelers. They
frequently set up a base of operations, then ambush travelers
on nearby roads.

DC20 Glossary
General
Area Attack: An Attack against creatures within a specified
area, such as an Aura, Cone, or Line. These Attacks don’t
have DisADV against creatures within 1 Space of the
attacker.
Monsters
(Auto) [reaction’s name]: These types of Reactions
happen automatically, without a creature needing to spend
AP or even being able to spend AP (such as when it’s
Incapacitated). They’re often used for on-death Actions
and rare interactions that don’t require the creature’s input
to activate.
Round Action: Unless the monster is Incapacitated, these
Actions happen at the start of the listed round of combat
before any creature’s turn.
[Damage] (Absorb): This creature has Immunity to this
damage type. When this damage type is dealt against the
creature, it regains HP equal to half the damage prevented.
Hovering Speed: A Flying Speed that allows the creature
to stay aloft even after gaining the Prone Condition.
Reaction Points (RP): At the end of a turn, the monster
can spend any number of Reaction Points (RP) to take a
number of Actions equal to the number of PCs occupying
that turn. Reaction Points (RP) are regained at start of the
round.

Creatures with a Burrow Speed can Burrow
