Introduction
When reading one of our Stat Blocks, you’ll want to keep
in mind the following assumptions and shortcuts used to
facilitate reading them at a glance during gameplay and
condensing the word count to be more straightforward and
less verbose. We’ll continue to iterate on this design, but it’s
a very practical update and standardization upon which we
will build going into Beta 0.10 and beyond.
You vs It
Monsters are always referred to as “You” in a Stat Block,
and creatures other than the titular monster are referred to
as “It” , and occasionally “Them” .
Reimagined Action
Presentation
Here is an example of where various pieces of information
go within our monster Actions.
- Actions like Dominate specify a [Check vs Save] in the
place of the [Attack type vs Defense].
Attacks & Checks vs Saves
(#) Name: [conditional targeting requirement], [Attack
type vs Defense], [Range of Action], [Area & Size],
[damage & damage type] and target makes [Save type].
Failure: Target is affected by an effect.
Contests
(#) Name: Contest, [Range of Action], [Monster Skill] vs
[Player Skill] or [Optional 2nd Skill]. Success: Target is
affected by an effect.
How To Read These Actions
The following will explain what each section of an Action
means and why it’s there. In general, Actions are designed
to be read left to right and present the information you’ll
need in the order you’ll need it whenever possible and
nonintrusive.
Conditional Targeting Requirement
This is presented first as it’s necessary to occur before an
Action can be used, typically some sort of Condition or
unique effect applied by a monster. Examples include “A
Grappled target” , “A Prone target” , and “Targets affected
by [effect]”
Your vs A target: “A Grappled target” refers to any
target that is Grappled, by you or another creature. “Your
Grappled target” specifically requires you to be the one
Grappling the target.
Attack Type vs Defense
Attack Types are defined in the game, but include 2
main distinctions:
- Area, Melee, or Ranged
- Martial or Spell
Every Attack is made against one of the target’s defenses:
Precision Defense (PD) or Area Defense (AD)
Range of an Action
The target, or point of origin, of an effect must be within
this range and no farther. Some Ranged Attacks have 2
numbers, designating that it has a normal and long Range.
Area & Size
Areas have sizes determined by their shape and include: Arcs,
Auras, Cones, Cubes, Cylinders, Line, Spheres, & Walls.
Damage & Damage Type
Attacks deal an amount of damage of a certain type. If an
Attack deals more than 1 damage type, Heavy Hits and
other additional damage can be of either type.
Save Type
Attacks and other Actions can force targets to make a Save:
Might, Agility, Charisma, Intelligence, Physical, or Mental.
Remember that Physical and Mental Saves are used less
frequently compared to the others and that it allows a target
to choose its better Save in that particularly category.
Defense Update
Our changes to Precision and Area Defense come with a
need to relearn how to make monsters in a DC20 way, and
teach our testers how to do the same. The Monster Statistics
Table below defines the average values of a Monster from
Levels 1 to 10, the core experience of DC20 before PCs
enter Prestige Levels.
We’ve included the optional newcomer Levels of Novice
and Level 0 because we’ve published material at that
level and it’s something we plan to continue supporting
throughout development as the first-time introduction
for Players.
We’ll continue to refine this table as we continue development.
You should expect Monsters to be ever-so-slightly hardier
than a player of that level, but also to deal slightly less
damage on average. Scaling and Resource assumptions for
Monsters are something we’re still perfecting.

Base Damage: This is the expected Base Damage of a
single-target 1 AP Attack before Heavy Hits and Features
are accounted for.
Total Feature Power: This represents how much power
a Monster’s Features should be granting it. A value of 2
means that a Monster’s Features are equal in power to the
combined strength of 2 PC Features. We suggest comparing
Monster Features to Cheap Shot, Overload, Arcane Sigil,
Commander’s Call, Font of Inspiration, and Adaptive
Tactics as those are fairly straightforward in their expected
value and results. One of them likely corresponds to the vast
majority of Monster features.
- Example: A value of 2 could be 1 Feature that adds

of Cheap Shot). It could also be +1 damage to those
creatures (Cheap Shot) and one that generates a Help
Die once pre round (Adaptive Tactics).
Understanding PD
Precision Defense (PD) measures a creature’s ability to
avoid, deflect, or mitigate Attacks that target a specific
location on your body, such as a weapon strikes, ranged
projectiles, and rays of frost. It reflects a combination of
reflexes and tactical foresight, making it a crucial defense
against single-target assaults.
This Defense is often targeted by single-target Attacks.
However, the distinction between the two Defenses is
NOT drawn along that line. PD is designed as the Defense
creatures target when they Attack you specifically without
controlling you and/or your Space beforehand.
Multi-target PD Attacks include:
- Chain Lightning effects that have an Attack leaping from
1 target to another in a chain.
- A spell with multiple projectiles aimed at different targets.
Understanding AD
Area Defense (AD) measures a creature’s ability to
withstand Attacks that affect your Space rather than you
directly, such as explosions, cleaving weapon swings, and
other hard to avoid Attacks. Area Defense reflects your
ability to fight off less focused Area Attacks and push
through Attacks that you can’t get away from.
This Defense is often targeted by Attacks that target a
specific Area. However, there are many other ways a
creature might target your AD with a single-target Attack.
AD is designed as the Defense creatures target when they
Attack your Space with an Area of Effect, unless they’ve
otherwise controlled you within your Space or you’ve
entered a unique Space they can affect beforehand.
Single-target AD Attacks include:
- Crushing and Slamming a Grappled target.
- Attacks made against a creature that’s consumed,
engulfed, or otherwise at your mercy.
- Auto-applying damage like from the Destruction Blessing
of a Cleric.
- Aura Activations around a creature.
Beta Note: As we continue to explore this new direction,
we hope this update can help standardize these PD/AD
and Single-/Multi-target interactions. The goal is that the
distinction between DC20’s Defenses becomes natural for our
testers. We’re excited to see it continue developing into the
dynamic targeting system we think it can be.
Monster Roles Primer
I’m throwing out a lot of ideas and terms new to DC20
and want to make sure that these concepts are well defined
and understood by you, the reader. You’ll see a copy of
everything I’ve laid out for each role below and a quick
briefing of what the terms and sections are intended to
mean and how they should be used.
Monster roles describe how a creature can be used to its
maximum effectiveness in combat, allowing GMs to quickly
select a variety of creatures that synergize with one another
and play a different role in the battle. These roles don’t offer
any significant mechanical benefits or drawbacks, but they

do offer a glimpse into how a monster was designed and
how it might act in battle alongside others.
Using A Role
I’ve created a list of key words and terms that’ll be used
to indicate where a role’s strengths lie and what types of
countermeasures they’re susceptible to. These are listed
below as a role’s Strengths and Weaknesses.
HP: HP maximum and general hardiness
Defense: PD & AD, and general defensive options
Damage: Damage output and general offensive options
Close-Range: Close quarters combat, 3 Spaces or closer
Mid-Range: Mid-ranged combat, 3 Spaces to 10 Spaces
Long-Range: Long-ranged combat, 10 Spaces or farther
Mobility: Speed and movement options
Evasion: Ability to negate or avoid damage and Attacks
Crowd Control: Ability to hinder a creature’s movement
or Actions
Zone Control: Ability to shape or change the battlefield to
favor your side
Buffs: Ability to inspire, help, or enhance an ally
Debuffs: Ability to demoralize, hinder, or weaken an
enemy
Regeneration: Ability to recover HP for yourself or others
Tactics & Gameplay
This section is full of advice and examples that should teach
you the intended way to use these roles during combat.
They are, however, merely suggestions. Your creativity
and inspiration shouldn’t be confined by the suggested
approaches for each role.
Artillerists
Artillerists are powerful ranged combatants, dealing heavy
damage to foes before they reach the fight. They’re often
weak in melee and heavily rely on more resilient allies
to keep the enemy at a safe distance. Archers, damagefocused spellcasters, and even some dragons fall into the
Artillerist category.
Using an Artillerist
- Strengths: Damage, Long-Range, Zone Control
- Weaknesses: Close-Range, HP, Regeneration
Tactics & Gameplay
Distance and terrain are an Artillerist’s best friend! The
name of the game is to keep their enemies’ swords away
from their face. Pairing Artillerists with Brutes, Defenders,
or Skirmishers to distract or stand in front of the opponents
is a strong tactic. This role is often one of the main damage
sources of a combat and a prime first target for PCs with
ranged options. Make sure to use their range and ability to
position fairly freely to your advantage, using terrain and
elevation to its fullest.
A combat full of Artillerists without any meat shields in
the way works best when the terrain takes that role instead:
archers upon the walls of a castle, dragons spewing acid
from the skies, or shadow creatures hurling eldritch fire
from a distance while teleporting through that darkness.
Brutes
Brutes are hard-hitting bags of HP that wade through
enemy lines, crushing everything in their path. Brutes are
easy to hit, but they absorb damage like a sponge, flattening
anything dumb enough to get close. Ogres, Giants, and
Demons are commonly located in the Brute category.
Using a Brute
- Strengths: Damage, HP, Regeneration, Zone Control
- Weaknesses: Defense, Long-Range, Mobility
Tactics & Gameplay
Brutes are at their most dangerous when they’re up close
and personal. If they’re close enough to hit you, you’re
going to take a beating. Pairing Brutes with Artillerists,
Controllers, or Leaders to control the battlefield, slowing or
distracting enemies, and buffing the Brutes is a strong tactic.
This helps Brutes get up close and deal their heavy damage
and get into the backline of the opposing side.
A combat full of Brutes without any support is best when
there’s not much space for PCs to run, forcing them to get
close to the Brutes: a battle in an obscuring forest where the
foliage makes ranged combat a struggle, a narrow ravine
with one way in and one way out, or a small room with little
room to evade a rampaging Demon.

Controllers
Controllers are cunning foes that manipulate the battlefield
to the detriment of their enemies and benefit of their allies.
These types of creatures often rely on area-of-effect abilities
and debuffs. Many spellcasters, hags, Fey, and even serpents
are considered Controllers.
Using a Controller
- Strengths: Crowd Control, Debuffs, Zone Control
- Weaknesses: Close-Range, Damage, HP
Tactics & Gameplay
Controllers are the masters of battlefield control. They
can lock down individual targets or entire areas. Pairing
Controllers with Defenders, Lurkers, and Skirmishers
tends to work well. They thrive when enabling others to
deal massive amounts of damage and keeping themselves
out of harm’s way. Controllers are often the first targets in
any fight, making it vital to give your side of the combat
some means of protecting them.
A combat full of Controllers is less of a fight to the death
and more a game of toying with the PCs or creating an
interesting moment: Fey tricksters “testing” the PCs before
an audience with their queen, a mage’s guild attempting
to slow down the PCs so their leader can flee, or an
exploration encounter full of oozes slowly drowning and
consuming the PCs. Controllers can also take advantage of
hazardous terrain and effects to potentially make encounters
even deadlier.
Defenders
Defenders are infantry-focused combatants with high
Defenses, moderate HP, and acceptable offensive
capabilities. The job of the Defender is to protect the
backline and keep the enemy in place. The Defender is
the core antithesis of the Brute as many of its features force
enemy creatures to attack it or stop its enemies from moving
away. Bodyguards, knights, and guardian constructs are
good examples of Defenders.
Using a Defender
- Strengths: Close-Range, Crowd Control, Debuffs,
Defense
- Weaknesses: Damage, Long-Range
Tactics & Gameplay
Defenders excel when presented with the opportunity to
stand in front of targets and hinder their mobility. They
find amazing value when paired with Artillerists, Lurkers,
and Supports. While other roles may present a threat on
their own, Defenders specialize in fortifying their allies
while hindering their enemies. You’ll want to have them
constantly reposition and stay between their backline and
the enemy’s frontline.
A combat full of defenders is probably not likely to occur
very often. However, one might find themselves needing to
build a “hold the line” type moment. The overall lack of
offensive capabilities and utility tends to leave Defenders
needing the assistance of other roles to create dynamic and
engaging encounters that tell compelling stories.
Leaders
Leaders are the beating heart of the enemy force, a
commander that buffs and heals their allies while
simultaneously putting pressure on the enemy. They shine
in tactical combat, repositioning allies and giving them
additional options in battle. Warlords, battle clerics, officers,
and generals are great examples of Leaders.
Using a Leader
- Strengths: Buffs, Debuffs, Regeneration, Zone Control
- Weaknesses: Crowd Control, Evasion, Long-range
Tactics & Gameplay
Leaders are true generals, and as such, they’re meant to
command allies and lead them to victory, rather than
fighting as lone wolves. Many leaders like to stand between
the frontline and backline of a combat, protecting the
weaker members with their bodies while empowering their
damage dealers or augmenting their tanks. Their toolkit
tends to revolve around auras, enabling allies to move and
attack, and generating small advantages round after round
that slowly tilt the scales in their favor.
A combat full of Leaders, much like some of the other roles,
is fairly unlikely. However, unlike Defenders, Leaders
have interesting ways of engaging with each other that
could make for unique encounters: a group of monks that
each have their own mantra aura to empower the others,
a raiding party of hobgoblins that are incredibly strong so
long as they’re all alive, or a series of symbiotic oozes that
function as one organism with many different bodies.
Lurkers
Lurkers are dangerous strikers that move in and out of danger,
delivering devastating blows before they return to safety.
They remain at the edge of the fight, counterbalancing their
mediocre HP and Defenses with their unequaled damage.
Black dragons, assassins, and invisible stalkers are good
examples of Lurkers.
Using a Lurker
- Strengths: Damage, Evasion, Mobility
- Weaknesses: Crowd Control, Defense, HP, Zone
Control
Tactics & Gameplay
Lurkers are at their best when they can get in close to
deal damage, and then quickly get away. When paired
with Controllers, Leaders, and Supports they can use the

controlling effect, positioning, and buffs of their allies to
get the drop on their enemies. However, Lurkers are easily
taken out when they have nowhere to go and no support.
When they have allies that can take the heat off of them,
they can swoop in and deal massive damage with little fear.
A combat full of Lurkers without anyone to distract their
targets works best when the terrain provides ample space
throughout which they can hide and maneuver: a back
alleyway where assassins can dive into cover, a forest where
goblins can weave through shrubs and behind trees, or a
swamp where a black dragon can maneuver under the water.
Skirmishers
Skirmishers are often fast and versatile creatures that focus
on putting their enemies in awkward positions, kiting
their foes, or acting as an annoyance to the back line of
an adventuring party. The versatility of the Skirmisher
often allows it to fight at range and in melee, using its
mobility to keep away from bigger threats and reach easier
targets. Scouts, goblins, and soldiers are good examples of
Skirmishers.
Using a Skirmisher
- Strengths: Damage, Defense, Mobility
- Weaknesses: Long-range, Zone Control
Tactics & Gameplay
Skirmishers are best when they can maneuver freely, getting
up close to dish out damage to the weakest targets. They
operate their best when they’re backed up by Artillerists,
Brutes, or Leaders that can complement their damage
output and apply pressure that allows them to single out
their enemies.
A combat against a squad of Skirmishers would be most
thrilling in an area that hampers the PCs movement and
actions while leaving the Skirmishers unaffected. This
could be a group of goblins ambushing the PCs while
moving through small tunnels, bandits in a fortress set
with traps, or vampires attacking from the shadows in a
mansion and darting away by climbing up the walls and
into hidden areas.
Supports
These creatures are designed to heal allies and remove
negative effects, but often help their allies to ensure
the effectiveness of those actions. Supports rarely have
threatening offensive capabilities, and are used to maximize
the survivability of their allies. Apothecaries, dryads, and
battlefield healers are good examples of Support creatures.
Using a Support
- Strengths: Buffs, Crowd Control, Debuffs, Regeneration
- Weaknesses: Damage, Defense, Mobility
Tactics & Gameplay
Supports are fairly frail and useless on their own, but a
force to be reckoned with when in a group setting. Just one
Support is enough to dramatically affect the damage output
and sustainability of their side. Because of this reliance on
others, Supports are best paired with Artillerists, Brutes,
Lurkers, and Skirmishers. These roles are prime targets for
their enhancements and healing effects.
A combat against a group of Support creatures would likely
be a bit boring, as Supports don’t have the damage output
or battlefield control to make for an interesting narrative
or a challenging fight. Supports are meant to enhance
other creatures, and can even make good candidates for
temporary NPC allies that put the emphasis on the PCs.
Crafting
Encounters
One of the hardest parts about running any game like DC20
is building encounters that are well-balanced and feel
appropriately challenging. This system addresses that by
using a level-based system for combat that roughly mirrors
the Levels of your PCs.
Encounter Budgets
You can use the a Monster’s Level, which is a significant
challenge for a PC of the same Level, to accurately and
quickly build an encounter. This is done by using a point
system that stems from totaling the levels on both sides of
the combat. The PCs’ Level total is referred to as the GM’s
Encounter Budget. A combat is considered a significant
challenge when it approaches or exceeds this budget.
Encounter Budget: PC Level times the number of PCs.
- 4 Level 5 PCs = 20 Levels
To start, you take the Level of the PCs and multiply it
by the number of PCs in the party. Then you add up the
Levels of all of the monsters in the encounter, and compare
the 2 results. For example, 4 PCs that are 15th Level would
have an encounter budget of 60. 3 Monsters of 15th level
would give you 45 points and, since 45 is less than 60,
you know the combat won’t be very hard. In this system,
specific types of creatures have multipliers to their point
totals, as follows:
- Apex: Twice the Creature’s Level
- Legendary: Quadruple the Creature’s Level
When the total Monster Levels of an encounter exceeds
your Encounter Budget, the encounter begins moving
towards Hard, and eventually Deadly. If the monsters’
total is equal to or lower than the Encounter Budget, the
encounter is Medium, Easy, or Trivial.

Combat Difficulty
Combat encounters range from Deadly to Trivial. However,
the intention isn’t to throw a series of two or three times
deadly encounters at your party just so that they don’t
steamroll through them. In this new, revised system, Deadly
encounters are truly that: DEADLY. We all know that
parties outnumbered in the action economy of a fight have
it rougher. So we’ve applied that logic to monster levels as
well to make combat design more intuitive.
Deadly
Everyone’s wounded, multiple people are going to 0 HP, 50/50
chance someone dies
Deadly encounters are often not designed to be thrown
at the party outside of climactic final scenes and fights.
Parties will often find themselves in a Deadly encounter
through poor choices and a failure to plan their approach
to a situation properly. There’s a place and time for Deadly
encounters, particularly during adventuring days and
exploration scenes that only have 1 fight. However, even
in those situations, Hard is often tough enough unless this
fight is a significant story moment.
Hard
PCs are heavily wounded, someone’s going to 0 HP, significant
chance someone dies
Hard encounters are the staple “set-piece encounters” that
most GMs are used to. These are significant challenges
that carry a very real chance of character death and PCs
dropping to 0 HP. A group can likely only survive 1
Hard encounter at a time, and will definitely need to rest
afterwards before moving on. Otherwise, they’ll have to
retreat and return when they’re rested.
These fights are intended to be a challenge worth avoiding if
possible. Players are encouraged to tackle Hard encounters
with a certain degree of planning and strategy in order to
come out with everyone alive and well. Ambushing the
enemy, applying buffs before engaging, and choosing
the location of the engagement are all vital to winning a
Hard encounter.
Medium
PCs are lightly wounded, 50/50 chance someone goes to 0 HP,
small chance someone dies
Medium encounters are the average encounters within
an adventure. They carry a risk and appropriate level of
danger without the grave penalties of imminent death. A
party can likely handle 3 medium encounters a day without
much trouble provided they have time to rest in between.
Adding in other challenges, a tougher fight, or various
resource draining elements to an adventuring day will
reduce this number.
Most encounters in a campaign will likely fall right around
this threshold. Think of Medium as a home base. It’s
always a good fight to throw at the group when you don’t
want something that’s likely to kill them, but still provides
a significant challenge.
Easy
Some PCs are lightly wounded, small chance someone goes to 0
HP, unlikely someone dies
Easy encounters will leave the party with some small
wounds and tax their HP totals slightly. The party isn’t
likely to feel overly taxed and threatened at any point unless
luck really goes against them. These fights tend to be the
minor battles that fill the spaces in between more climactic
moments. The party isn’t likely to need a significant rest or
recovery period after an easy encounter unless one of the
PCs was particularly targeted during the engagement.
Many random encounters, battles within the side rooms of a
dungeon, and introductory battles to a new faction or enemy
type during an adventure would fall under this category.
Easy encounters can still provide unique challenges and are
prime candidates for incorporating secondary objectives.
The goblins might be of little threat, but two of them are
running around town slaughtering the innocent while the
combat goes on, forcing the PCs to split up.

Trivial
PCs are barely wounded, unlikely someone goes to 0 HP, almost
impossible someone dies
Trivial encounters are much more common in social and
exploration segments of a campaign, short interludes in
a much larger scene or story. However, Trivial combat
encounters can crop up when the party ambushes a lone
guard or two and stacks the deck in their favor dramatically.
A party can likely take on a functionally infinite number of
Trivial encounters assuming they can rest and heal between
them. While it’s very unlikely that you’d ever use more than
a single Trivial encounter during an adventuring day, there
might be certain moments that require a series of them.
Consider this difficulty for your wave-defense, king of
the hill, or ticking trap scenarios that continually send
in small groups of enemies to complicate the encounter.
A Trivial combat is often more than just a fight. It can
reveal interesting bits of your world’s lore, drop clues for
the coming challenges, introduce new mechanics for this
adventure, or be a part of another type of encounter. If you
assume that a couple of the PCs are tied up solving a trap or
puzzle, the difficulty of a Trivial encounter can quickly rise
to Easy or Medium.
Combat Difficulty Breakdown
Once you have your Encounter Budget for a party
determined, you can compare it to the number of Monster
Levels in a combat to determine its difficulty. Use the
rubric below to see where your planned encounter falls on
the difficulty scale.
Evaluating A Combat’s
Monster Level Total
Deadly: Encounter Budget + 2 PCs worth of Levels
Hard: Encounter Budget + 1 PC worth of Levels
Medium: Encounter Budget
Easy: Encounter Budget - 1 PC worth of Levels
Trivial: Encounter Budget - 2 PCs worth of Levels
Example Encounters
For a Party of 4 Level 5 PCs, an Encounter Budget of 20
Levels, here’s how a few combats would rate:
Deadly: 6 Level 5 Monsters (30 total)
Hard: 4 Level 6 Monsters (24 total)
Medium: 5 Level 4 Monsters (20 total)
Easy: 3 Level 5 Monsters (15 total)
Trivial: 3 Level 3 Monsters (9 total)
- Trivial Encounters used during a Puzzle or Trap that
occupies 2 PCs per round can turn into Medium
Encounters (10 PC Levels vs 9 Monster Levels)
