## Combat Rules

### Combat Resources

#### Action Points

Action Points (AP) represent a character’s moment-tomoment readiness in both battle. Each character has a
maximum of 4 AP, which they can spend to perform
Actions or Reactions. Whether it’s striking with a blade,
casting a spell, or leaping from danger’s path, managing AP
wisely is key to surviving fast-paced encounters and making
the most of every opportunity.
Maximum AP
You have a maximum of 4 Action Points. You are considered
to be taking an Action when you spend 1 or more Action
Points. Once an Action Point is spent, it can’t be used again
until it’s regained.
Example: A PC has 4 AP. They spend 1 AP to make an Attack
with a Weapon they’re wielding. They decide to spend 3 AP
to improve their Attack: 1 AP to increase damage, 1 AP to
increase their range, and 1 AP to gain ADV on the Attack.
After spending all 4 AP, they can’t take any more Actions until
they regain their AP. See the “Martial Enhancements” on page
50 for more information on enhancing your Attacks.
Actions out of Combat Sidebar
When you are not in Combat, there is no
Initiative, so the GM decides how quickly
time flows. They set the pace and determine
how often creatures can take actions. In
calm situations, you might have all the time
you need, taking action after action without
limit. In situations where time is short (such as
a sneaking into a bandit’s hideout), the GM
may limit how many actions you can take to
help track the order and urgency of events.
You do not need to track movement the
same way you do in Combat.
For example, nobody is counting every single
Space someone walks while shopping in the
market. Similarly, you do not spend Action
Points out of Combat. You simply describe
what you are doing. However, the number
of Action Points you could spend in Combat
can be a useful guideline for how much effort
you can reasonably put into a single attempt
at an activity.
Beta Note: We will be adding or changing the rules around
Helping outside of Combat in a future update.

### Mana Points

Mana Points (MP) are the wellspring of magical power,
allowing Spellcasters to unleash potent spells and
supernatural effects. Unlike Stamina Points, MP can’t be
regained through action or effort during the day, instead
fully replenishing only after a Long Rest. However,
Spellcasters have a larger pool of MP than Martials do
SP, and each Mana Point produces a greater effect than
a Stamina Point, making every MP-fueled ability a more
powerful expenditure of energy.
MP Effects
Any ability or effect that costs MP is considered an MP
Effect. This includes Spells, Class Features, or Talents that
you spend MP to use.
Maximum MP
You don’t have Mana Points unless you choose a Spellcaster
Class (Bard, Cleric, Druid, Sorcerer, Spellblade*, Warlock,
and Wizard) or progress in the Spellcaster Path (see
“Character Paths” on page 192 for more information).
- Spellcaster Class: Spellcaster Classes have a maximum
amount of MP as shown in the Mana Points column of
their Spellcaster Class Table.
- Spellcaster Path: If you spend a Path point on the
Spellcaster Path, you gain maximum amount of MP as
shown on the Mana Points column of the Spellcaster
Path Table.
Mana Spend Limit
You can spend a maximum number of Mana Points to
produce an MP Effect equal to your Combat Mastery. This
limitation includes MP spent on Spell Enhancements and
other Features that require MP. This is called your Mana
Spend Limit (MSL).
Example: A level 6 Sorcerer (Combat Mastery of 3) can spend
up to 3 MP on any Spell they cast. They could cast a Spell
with a base cost of 3 MP, or they could cast a Spell with a
base cost of 1 MP and spend 2 MP on Spell Enhancements.
Alternatively, they could cast a Spell without an MP cost and
spend 3 MP on Spell Enhancements.
DC Tip: The Mana Spend Limit is separate from the Stamina
Spend Limit, meaning you could spend up to your MSL and
up to you SSL on the same action, such as when using the
Spellblade’s Spellstrike Feature.
Regaining MP
You regain all spent MP when you complete a Long Rest.

### Stamina Points

Stamina Points (SP) are a vital resource, empowering
Martial characters to perform heroic feats beyond the limits
of ordinary warriors. Each Martial Class has unique ways to
regain SP, whether through relentless aggression, strategic
maneuvers, or unwavering discipline, ensuring they can
continue to push the limits of Combat.
SP Effects
Any ability or effect that costs SP is considered an SP
Effect. This includes Maneuvers, Class Features, or Talents
that you spend SP to use.
Maximum SP
You don’t have Stamina Points unless you choose a Martial
Class (Barbarian, Champion, Commander, Hunter, Rogue,
Monk, and Spellblade*) or progress in the Martial Path (see
“Character Paths” on page 192 for more information).
- Martial Class: Martial Classes have a maximum amount
of SP as shown in the Stamina Points column of their
Martial Class Table.
- Martial Path: If you spend a Path point on the Martial
Path, you gain maximum amount of SP as shown on the
Stamina Points column of the Martial Path Table.
Stamina Spend Limit
You can spend a maximum number of Stamina Points to
perform an SP Effect equal to your Combat Mastery. This
limitation includes SP spent on Maneuver and Attack
Enhancements and other Features that require SP. This is
called your Stamina Spend Limit (SSL).
Example: A level 6 Barbarian (Combat Mastery of 3) can
spend up to 3 SP on any SP Effect they perform. They could
perform a Maneuver with a base cost of 3 SP, or they could
perform a Maneuver with a base cost of 1 SP and spend 2
SP on Maneuver Enhancements. Alternatively, they could
perform a Maneuver without an SP cost and spend 3 SP on
Maneuver Enhancements.
DC Tip: The Stamina Spend Limit is separate from the Mana
Spend Limit, meaning you could spend up to your SSL and
up to you MSL on the same action, such as when using the
Spellblade’s Spellstrike Feature.
Regaining SP
You regain all spent SP when Combat ends and when you
complete a Short Rest.
Martial Stamina Regen: You can regain SP during Combat
in a variety of ways depending on the Stamina Regen
benefit granted by your Class or from the Martial Path
(see the “Character Paths” on page 192chapter for more
information).
Example: The Champion Class regains SP by performing
Maneuvers, the Barbarian regains SP by taking or scoring
Heavy, Brutal, and Critical Hits, and the Rogue regains SP by
hitting creatures that are affected by Conditions.

### Grit Points

Characters with a high Charisma Attribute have a strong
force of will. Grit Points represent a Character being able to
push through and go beyond their limits in difficult situations.
Maximum Grit Points
You have a maximum number of Git Points equal to 2 +
your Charisma (minimum of 0 Grit Points).
Spending Grit Points
Bearing Damage: When you’re Hit by an Attack, you can
spend 1 or more Grit Points to reduce the damage you take
by 1 per Grit Point spent.
Save Resolve: When you make a Save, you can spend Grit
Points to grant yourself ADV on the Save. You must do so
before you roll any dice as part of making the Save.
Regaining Grit Points
You regain all spent Grit Points when you complete
a Long Rest.

#### Taking an Action

A creature can take an Action on its turn by spending
Action Points (AP). The AP cost of taking an Action is
listed in the Action’s description.
Minor Action
Once per turn, you can perform up to 2 of the following
tasks without spending AP. You can’t take Actions (such as
the Attack Action) between these 2 tasks.
- Open or Close a Door.
- Grab or Release an Item.
- Retrieve or Stow an Item.
- Draw or Sheathe a Weapon.
Additional Minor Actions: You can spend 1 AP to perform
a Minor Action again during the same turn.

### Types of Actions

A creature can take the following Actions on its turn:
Attack, Move, Disengage, Dodge, Grapple, Help, Hide,
Object, Shove, and Spell Action.

#### Offensive Actions

Attack
You can spend 1 AP to make 1 Attack Check.
Disarm
You can spend 1 AP to make an Attack Check contested
by the target’s Athletics, Acrobatics, or Trickery Check
(target’s choice), the target has ADV if they’re holding
the object with 2 hands, you have DisADV if the target
is larger than you. You can’t Disarm a creature that’s 2
Sizes larger than you. Success: The targeted object falls
into an unoccupied space of your choice within 1 Space of
the creature.
Grapple
Using a free hand, you can spend 1 AP to attempt to
Grapple another creature within 1 Space. Make an
Athletics Check contested by the opposing creature’s
Acrobatics or Athletics Check. You or the target may have
ADV or DisADV on your Check based on each other’s size.
Success: The creature is Grappled by you.
Moving the Target
You can move the target to any Space adjacent to you by
spending your own Movement to do so. Alternatively,
when you move, you can move the target with you, but
you’re considered Slowed (Every 1 Space you move costs
an extra 1 Space of movement).
Multiple Grapplers: If you attempt to move or throw a
target (see “Throw Creature” on page 57) that’s also
Grappled by 1 or more other creatures, you must make a
Contested Athletics Check against all the other Grapplers.
Success: You move or throw the target as normal and break
the Grapple of all other creatures who were also Grappling
that target.
Ending a Grapple
Escape Grapple: The target can spend 1 AP to attempt
to free itself from the Grapple. When it does, it makes an
Acrobatics or Athletics Check contested by your Athletics
Check. Success: The Grapple immediately ends.
Incapacitated Grappler: If you become Incapacitated, the
Grapple immediately ends.
Forced Movement: If an effect attempts to forcibly move
the target beyond your reach, you make the Check or Save
instead of the target. If the effect targets both you and the
target, you make 1 Check or Save for both of you. Success:
The targets of the effect are not moved. Failure: The
Grapple immediately ends, and the targets of the effect are
moved.
Falling: If the target begins falling (and you don’t), the
Grapple ends if you can’t carry the target’s weight. If you
can carry its weight, you hold it suspended in the air.
Shove
You can spend 1 AP to attempt to push a creature within 1
Space of you. Make an Athletics Check contested by the
target’s Acrobatics or Athletics Check. Success: You push
the creature 1 Space away from you or to its left or right.
Success (each 5): Push up to 1 additional Space.
Knock Prone: After the result, you can choose to reduce
the total distance the target is pushed by 1 Space to knock
them Prone instead.
DC Tip: If you shove a creature into a target, follow the rules
for hitting the target in the Collision section.
Tackle
If you move at least 2 Spaces in a straight line, you can spend
1 AP to attempt to Tackle a creature that’s your Size or
smaller. Make an Athletics Check contested by the target’s
Acrobatics or Athletics Check. Success: You Grapple the
target and you both move 1 Space in the same direction
you’re moving then immediately fall Prone. Success (each
5): +1 Space moved.

#### Defensive Actions

Disengage
You can spend 1 AP to impose DisADV on Opportunity
Attacks made against you until the start of your next turn.
Full Disengage: When you take the Disengage Action,
you can spend an additional 1 AP to become Immune to
Opportunity Attacks until the start of your next turn.
Dodge
You can spend 1 AP to impose DisADV on the next Attack or
Grapple made against you before the start of your next turn.
Full Dodge: When you take the Dodge Action, you can
spend an additional 1 AP to impose DisADV on all Attacks
or Grapples made against you until the start of your next
turn.
Hide
You can spend 1 AP to attempt to Hide from 1 or more
creatures that can’t see you (Unseen). Make a Stealth
Check against any opposing creatures’ Passive Awareness.
Success: You become Hidden from creatures whose
Passive Awareness you beat (making you Unseen and
Unheard by them). You remain Hidden until you make a
noise louder than a whisper, make an Attack, cast a Spell
with a Verbal Component, or a creature takes the Search
Action and successfully locates you.
See “Hidden Creatures” on page 163 for more on hiding.
DC Tip: When you take the Hide Action and beat an enemy’s
Passive Awareness, you only become Hidden from that
creature because it can’t see or hear you (you are both
Unseen and Unheard). However, if your Stealth Check beats
one enemy’s Awareness Score but not another’s, then you
are Hidden from the first enemy, but you’re not Hidden from
the second enemy.

#### Utility Actions

Move
You can spend 1 AP to move up to your Speed in Spaces
(default of 5). You choose where to move, and can break
up your movement by moving before and after taking
a different Action. You can’t end your turn in a Space
occupied by another creature.
Example: A creature with 4 AP can take the Move Action to
move 1 Space, take the Attack Action, move 1 Space again (2
total Spaces), take the Attack Action again, and then move 2
Spaces again (4 total Spaces).
Help
You can spend 1 AP to grant another creature a d8 Help Die
that lasts until the start of your next turn. Upon granting the
Help Die, you must declare which creature you’re Helping
and the type of Check you’ll be aiding them with, while
meeting the following conditions:
Attack: You declare 1 target for the Attack. You must be
within 1 Space of the Attacker or the target of the Attack.
While the Help Die lasts, it can be added to an Attack made
against the target.
Skill or Trade Check: You declare a type of Skill or Trade
Check. You describe how you’re Helping them and must
do so with a Skill or Trade that you have at least 1 Mastery
Level in. You can use the same Skill or Trade or a different
one.
The Help Die can only be used to aid the type of Check
declared and disappears once it’s used.
Multiple Help Penalty: Once you take the Help Action,
each time you take the Help Action again before the end
of your turn, your Help Die decay by 1 step, to a minimum
of a d4 (d8 > d6 > d4). These Help Dice only decay when
using the Help Action. Help Die granted by other sources
decay independently of any Help Dice grant through the
Help Action.
Example: A Barbarian is about to wrestle an alligator and
will need to make an Athletics Checks. A Hunter with Mastery
in the Animal Skill can grant the Barbarian a Help Die to add
to their Athletics Check made to wrestle the alligator.
DC Tip: When taking the Help Action, the GM may allow the
PC to contribute to a Skill or Trade Check by using something
other than their own Skill or Trade Mastery. If the PC is a
Wizard, they may try to use a Spell to contribute to another
creature’s Skill or Trade Check, but only if the GM decides
that it makes sense to do so.
Object
You can spend 1 AP to perform 1 of the following
object interactions:
- Drink a Potion or Administer a Potion to another Creature.
- Attempt to Lock or Unlock a Lock.
- Make a Trickery Check to activate or disable a trap or
other mechanism.
- Transfer an Item to or from another Creature (only 1 of
the two creatures spends 1 AP).
- Throw an Item to location you can see up to 5 Spaces
away.
Spell
You can spend 1 or more AP to cast a Spell that you know.
If the Spell has a Mana Point requirement, you must spend
that much MP to cast the Spell.
Sustained Action
You must spend 1 AP at the start of each of your turns to
Sustain an effect until the start of your next turn. You can’t
Sustain an effect while Dazed. When you become Dazed,
any effect you’re Sustaining ends. You can choose to end
the effect at anytime for free.
Outside of Combat: You can only Sustain 1 effect at a time
outside of Combat. If you attempt to Sustain a second effect
while already Sustaining a first, the first effect immediately
ends.

#### Skill Based Actions

A creature can take the following Skill Actions on its turn:
Analyze Creature, Calm Animal, Combat Insight, Conceal,
Feint, Intimidate, Investigate, Jump, Medicine, Mounted
Defense, Pass Through, or Search.
Analyze Creature
You can spend 1 AP to attempt to recall or discern some
information about a creature that you can see or hear. Make
a DC 10 Knowledge Check.
Success: You learn a piece of lore about the creature.
Success (each 5): You learn 1 creature statistic (PD,
AD,
Attacks,
Abilities,
Resistances,
Vulnerabilities,
Immunities, etc.)
DC Tip: There are 5 types of Knowledge Checks related
to creature types: Arcana (Constructs, Monstrosities,
and Oozes), History (Dragons, Giants, and Humanoids),
Nature (Beasts, Elementals, Fey, and Plants), Occultism
(Aberrations, Fiends, and Undead), and Religion (Celestials).
If a PC has a custom Knowledge Trade they think might help
in the situation, they can ask the GM to use that Knowledge
Trade instead.
Calm Animal
You can spend 1 AP to attempt to beguile a Beast that can
see or hear you. Make an Animal Check contested by the
target’s Repeated Charisma Save.
Success: The animal is Taunted by you for 1 minute or
until you target it with a harmful Attack, Spell, or other
effect. Success (5): It’s instead Intimidated. Success (10):
It’s also Charmed.
Combat Insight
You can spend 1 AP to attempt to discern the course
of actions a creature might take on its next turn. Make
an Insight Check contested by the target’s Trickery or
Influence Check (its choice).
Success: You learn the target’s emotional state and whether
it plans to make an Attack, cast a Spell, or flee combat
during its next turn. Success (5): You know who the
creature is likely to target with a harmful ability. Success
(10): You know which ability the creature plans to use.
DC Tip: If the target is a PC, its player tells you what the
creature plans to do. If the target is an NPC, the GM tells you
what the creature plans to do.
Conceal
You can spend 1 AP to attempt to hide an object on yourself
or in nearby foliage, debris, or decor to render it Hidden.
Make a contested Trickery Check against the Passive
Awareness of creatures that can see you.
Success: The object is Hidden from any creature whose
Passive Awareness you beat.
Feint
You can spend 1 AP to make a Trickery Check contested
by the Insight Check of a creature that can see you
within 3 Spaces.
Success: The next Attack against the target before the start
of your next turn has ADV and deals +1 damage.
Intimidate
You can spend 1 AP to attempt to intimidate a creature that
can see or hear you within 10 Spaces. Make an Intimidation
Check contested by the target’s Charisma Save. Success:
The target is Intimidated by you for 1 Round.
Investigate
You can spend 1 AP to attempt to uncover a concealed
object on a creature, a secret compartment, or the intended
function of a mechanism within 1 Space of you.
Concealed Objects: You can attempt to uncover any objects
concealed on a creature. Make an Investigation Check
contested by the target’s Trickery Check.
Success: You know the location of any concealed object on
the creature.
Secret Compartments: You can attempt to uncover any
secret comportments. Make an Investigation Check against
the discovery DC of any secret compartments.
Success: You discover the location of any secret
compartments whose discovery DC you beat.
Discern Mechanism: You can attempt to discern the
functionality of a mechanism (the effect of a trap, how
to open a secret door, or activate a device). Make an
Investigation Check.
Success: You learn how the mechanism works and the
methods to activate and disable it (if any).
Extend Jump
When you perform a Jump, you can spend 1 AP to
increase your Jump Distance for the Jump. Make a DC 10
Acrobatics Check. Failure: Your Jump Distance increases
by 1. Success: Your Jump Distance increases by 2. Success
(each 5): +1 Jump Distance.
DC Tip: Remember, your Long Jump Distance equals your
Jump Distance in Spaces, and your High Jump distance equals
your Jump Distance in feet.
Medicine
You can spend 1 AP to touch a creature and tend to its
wounds. Make a DC 10 Medicine Check. Success: You
end 1 stack of Bleeding on the target. Success (each 5):
You end 1 additional stack of Bleeding on the target.

Mounted Defense
You can spend 1 AP to maneuver a mount you’re riding to
avoid danger. Make a DC 10 Animal Check.
Success: The mount’s PD increases by 2 until the start of
your next turn. Success (5): +2 PD. Success (10): +4 PD.
Pass Through
You can spend 1 AP to attempt to move through a Space
occupied by a hostile creature that’s within 1 size of
you. Make a contested Martial Check against the target.
Success: You can move through the creature’s Space as if it
were Difficult Terrain (Slowed while moving through the
area). Success (5): You can move through the creature’s
space without it counting as Difficult Terrain.
Search
You can spend 1 AP to attempt to locate 1 or more Hidden
creatures and concealed objects within your Line of Sight.
Hidden Creatures: You attempt to locate any Hidden
creatures in the area. Make an Awareness Check against
the Stealth Check of any Hidden creatures. Success: You
know the location of any Hidden creature whose Stealth
Check you beat until the end of your turn. Knowing a
creature’s location doesn’t grant you the ability to see it.
Hidden Objects: You attempt to locate any Hidden objects
in the area. Make an Awareness Check against the DC to
discover any concealed objects (such as traps, secret doors,
or hidden items). Success: You discover the location of any
Hidden object whose discovery DC you beat.
DC Tip: Without obstruction, a PC’s line of sight could reach
the horizon. You might ask your PC to make their Awareness
Check with Disadvantage to detect a creature that’s far away,
such as beyond 10 Spaces.
Taunt
You can spend 1 AP to attempt to taunt a creature that
can see or hear you within 10 Spaces. Make an Influence
Check contested by the target’s Charisma Save. Success:
The target is Taunted by you for 1 Round.

### Advanced Actions

#### Held Actions

Sometimes the circumstances aren’t exactly right for the
action you want to take. When that happens, you can
choose to wait until just the right moment by Holding your
Action. To Hold your Action, you must declare an Action
you want to take and state a Trigger. Once you do so, you
can’t change the Action or Trigger.
Declare Action: When you declare the Action, you must
immediately spend the Action Points required to take that
Action. The Multiple Check Penalty from this turn applies
to any Checks you make as part of this Reaction.
State Trigger: The Trigger must be an observable event,
such as “I want to wait until an enemy moves into range to
make an Attack against them” , “I want to wait until I see
my ally get out of range before I shoot the barrel filled with
explosives” , or something else.
Take Reaction: If the stated Trigger occurs at any time
before the start of your next turn, you can take the declared
Action as a Reaction. If the Reaction requires you to spend
any additional resources (such as Stamina Points or Mana
Points) you spend them now. Your Reaction interrupts the
events stated in the Trigger, allowing you to act before
the events resolve. If the Trigger never occurs, then the
declared Action can’t be taken and the AP spent is wasted.
Example: It’s your turn and you have 2 AP remaining to
spend, but you really want to work together with an ally. You
tell the GM that you want to spend 2 AP on Held Actions.
You declare that you will be taking the Move Action and the
Attack Action and you spend 2 AP right now. You also state
the Trigger for these Held Actions is “your ally is in melee
range of an enemy” . Once the Trigger occurs you take those
2 Actions immediately. You take a Move Action to move to the
creatures and then you make an Attack Action on a creature.
If the creatures are too far away for 1 Move Action (1 AP),
then you would only be able to move as far as you can and
the Attack Action (1 AP) is wasted since you were not close
enough to make an Attack. You can’t convert the 1 AP spent
on the Attack Action into another Move Action, since that’s
not what you declared.
DC Tip: PCs can set triggers to be something less specific
like, “I’m going to shoot an arrow at the next enemy I see.”
Just make sure the PC and the GM are on the same page on
what the intent of what they’re wanting to do is for their
Reactions Trigger.

### Reactions

Reactions are Actions that a creature can take during
another creature’s turn. A creature can’t take a Reaction on
their own turn, unless the Reaction allows them to do so in
response to another creature’s Reaction. Reactions are not
affected by the Multiple Check Penalty.

#### Taking a Reaction

A creature can take a Reaction on another creature’s
turn provided it meets the requirements to do so. The
requirements to take a Reaction are listed in the Reaction’s
Prerequisite and Trigger statements. You can only take 1
Reaction per Trigger.
Example 1: When a creature leaves a Martial PC’s reach, they
would only be able to make 1 Opportunity Attack against
the creature (they could still spend AP to perform Martial
Enhancements with their Opportunity Attack).
Example 2: When an Attack is made within 1 Space of a
Martial PC, the PC could take the Parry Maneuver against
the Attack (1st trigger). If the Attack still Hits (2nd trigger), the
PC could take the Protection Maneuver to share the damage
with the target.
Reaction Requirements
Prerequisite: A prerequisite states the Feature (Martial
Feature, Spellcasting Feature, or a different Feature)
required to gain access to the Reaction.
Trigger: A trigger states the conditions that must be met in
order for a creature to take the Reaction.
Spending Resources
If a Reaction has a resource cost (Action Points, Mana
Points, Stamina Points, etc.), a creature must spend those
resources to take the Reaction.
Regaining Resources: Reactions give a creature an
opportunity to spend Action Points during another
creature’s turn. If a creature spends Action Points, they’ll
have less Action Points to spend on their next turn. Any
spent Action Points are not regained until the end of a
creature’s turn.
Example: If you take a Reaction to spend 1 Action Point,
on your next turn you will only have 3 Action Points to
use (4 -1 = 3).
DC Tip: If you somehow manage to spend all 4 of your AP
before your turn, then you would have 0 AP at the start of
your next turn. You wouldn’t be able to do anything, so your
turn would immediately end and you’d get your 4 AP back.
Multiple Reactions: A creature can spend Action Points
to take multiple Reactions provided they meet all the
conditions and can spend enough resources to do so.

#### Types of Reactions

Opportunity Attack
Prerequisite: Martial Path
Trigger: A creature you can see within your Melee Range
uses its movement to leave your Melee Range, draws a
Weapon or Spell Focus, picks an item up off the ground, or
takes the Object Action.
Reaction: You can spend 1 AP to make a Melee Martial
Attack against the provoking creature.
DC Tip: You can spend additional AP to gain ADV or to
perform Maneuvers with the Attack.

#### Spell Duel

Prerequisite: Spellcasting Path
During a Spell Duel, the creature casting the Spell is referred
to as the Caster and the opposing creature is referred to as
the Challenger.
If multiple creatures attempt to initiate a Spell Duel, one of
them must be chosen as the Challenger, while the others
assist them through Combo Casting. Additional creatures
can also choose to assist the Caster when a Spell Duel is
initiated (see “Combo Spellcasting” on page 67 for more
information).
Trigger: You see an MP Effect being cast, provided the
Caster or a target is within 5 Spaces of you.
Reaction: You can spend an amount of AP and 1 or more
MP (up to your Mana Spend Limit) to attempt to stop the
Caster’s MP Effect. The amount of AP spent is equal to
the base AP cost of the MP Effect you’re trying to stop.
Contest
Make a Spell Check contested by the Spell Check or Spell
Attack that the Caster makes to cast their MP Effect. If
the Caster’s MP Effect doesn’t require a Check, they have
to make a Spell Check as a Reaction as part of the Spell
Duel. For the purposes of the Spell Duel, the Caster and
Challenger each gain a bonus to their Check equal to
twice the MP spent they spent respectively.
Beta Note: Since enemies don’t have MP costs for their MP
Effects, you can assume their MP Effects cost an amount of
MP equal to half their Level (rounded up).

Outcomes
Success: The Caster’s MP Effect fails and has no effect.
Failure: The Caster’s MP Effect succeeds and takes effect.
The Check bonus gained from MP spent doesn’t apply to
the MP Effect’s Success and Failure Statements.
Tie: The Caster’s MP Effect fails, has no effect, and you
each roll on the Wild Magic Surge Table. The effect from
the table lasts until 1 Round has passed.
Resources Lost: Whatever the result, each creature still

makes a Spell Check. The Caster gets a +4 bonus to their
Attack Check (2 MP spent) and the Challenger gets a +2
bonus to their Spell Check (1 MP spent). Should the Caster
win the Contest, their MP Effect takes effect but they don’t
include the +4 bonus from MP spent to determine if they
score a Heavy Hit or higher.

### Martial Chapter

Martial characters in DC20 don’t merely fight - they wage
war with precision, discipline, and skill that elevates them
far beyond the capabilities of an ordinary sword-wielder.
Their prowess is represented through a specialized combat
system which includes:
- Maneuvers: Tactical feats of strength, speed, and finesse
used to control the battlefield.
- Enhancements:

### Maneuvers

Maneuvers are the signature techniques of martial
combatants; deliberate, high-impact actions that go beyond
basic attacks. Whether it’s a sweeping strike to knock
enemies back or a sudden burst of speed to reposition,
Maneuvers allow warriors to control the flow of battle with
skill and force.

#### Maneuvers Known

Martial Classes (Barbarian, Champion, Commander,
Hunter, Rogue, Monk, and Spellblade*) know a number
of Maneuvers as shown in the Maneuvers Known column
of their Class Tables, plus any Maneuvers learned from
Class Features, Talents, or Martial Path progression (see
“Character Paths” on page 192 for more information).

#### Maneuver Resources

Each Maneuver has a minimum number of Action Points
(AP) that must be spent to perform it. Many Maneuvers
also include a minimum number of Stamina Points (SP)
that must be spent to perform them. This is known as the
Base SP Cost of a Maneuver.

#### Action Points

Performing any Maneuver requires spending Action Points
(AP). Maneuvers that affect a single target typically cost 1
AP, while those that affect multiple targets usually cost 2 AP.

### Stamina Points

Some Maneuvers have an SP cost that you must spend to
perform them. The maximum amount of SP you can spend
on a single Maneuver must be equal to or lower than your
Stamina Spend Limit (see “Combat Resources” on page
41 for more information on Stamina Points).
DC Tip: Remember, your Stamina Spend Limit is equal to
your Combat Mastery.

#### Maneuver Characteristics

Duration
Maneuvers can either be instantaneous or continuous in
their duration, and some Maneuvers require you to Sustain
them in order to keep them going (see the “Sustained
Action” on page 44 for more information).
- Instantaneous: Instantaneous Maneuvers take effect the
moment they’re performed.
- Continuous: These Maneuvers last a specified amount of
time. They often end early when the affected creature
Succeeds on a subsequent Save against the effect, it or
another creature within 1 Space performs an Action to
end the effect, or some other method stated to end the
effect occurs (such as breaking line of sight).
- Sustained: Some effects require you to Sustain them or
they end.
Range
This is the distance within which a target of the Maneuver,
or the origin of its Area of Effect, must be. When selecting
the targets of your Maneuver, they must be within the
listed Range and fulfill any other listed requirement of the
Maneuver (such as being visible or capable of hearing you).
If the chosen Maneuver uses an Area of Effect to determine
its targets, the origin of the Maneuver must be within its
listed Range (see “Areas of Effect” on page 157 for more
information).

#### Performing a Maneuver

In order to perform a Maneuver, you must know the
Maneuver and spend the required amount of Resources
(Action Points and Stamina Points). When you perform
a Maneuver, you must make a Check (such as an Attack
Check or Martial Check) to determine the Maneuver’s
degree of success or failure. Your Check is compared against
the target’s Defense (such as PD or AD), the Maneuver’s
DC, or the target’s Contested Check.

### Enhancements

Enhancements amplify the impact of a character’s offense,
turning well-executed strikes into devastating blows. They
can increase damage, extend range, affect wider areas, or
inflict conditions, allowing martial characters to adapt to
the shifting demands of battle.

#### Maneuver Enhancements

Maneuver Enhancements allow you to spend additional AP
or SP to increase the Maneuver’s potency (such as damage,
duration, range, number of targets, or Area of Effect).
Maneuver Enhancements are specific to each Maneuver
and are included in a Maneuver’s description.

#### Performing a Maneuver

Enhancement
When you perform a Maneuver, you can spend additional
SP to add a Maneuver Enhancement with the Maneuver. If
the Maneuver requires a Check (such as an Attack Check
or Martial Check), you must choose to add the Maneuver
Enhancement before you make the Check. You can
perform the same Maneuver Enhancement multiple times
per Maneuver, unless stated otherwise.

#### Martial Enhancements

Martial Enhancements empower a character’s Martial
Attacks with added force and versatility. By spending
Action Points, a combatant can strike harder, reach farther,
or break a foe’s focus. These enhancements allow martial
characters to elevate even their simplest strikes into potent
tools of war, adapting on the fly to pressure, positioning, or
opportunity. Some Attack Enhancements require a certain
type of Weapon to perform (see “Equipment” on page 164
for more information).
Example: When you make an Attack with an Axe against 3
creatures using the Whirlwind Maneuver, you can spend 1
AP to add the Martial Enhancement: Damage and 1 AP to
add the Attack Enhancement: Bleeding available only to
Axes. 1 target of your choice takes +1 damage from the Attack
and another target of your choice (can be the same target)
must make a Save against your Save DC to avoid Bleeding.
Performing a Martial

Categorizing

### Maneuvers

#### Maneuver Types

There are several types of Maneuvers that allow you to
produce a variety of powerful effects.
Attack
Focused on delivering powerful, aggressive strikes, Attack
Maneuvers deal damage, and are the core of a martial
character’s offensive toolkit—optimized for impact,
precision, or area control.
Defense
Defense Maneuvers reinforce durability and survival.
Whether by parrying incoming blows, bracing against
force, or redirecting attacks, these Maneuvers let martial
characters hold the line and protect themselves or allies
under pressure.
Grapple
Grapple Maneuvers specialize in close-quarters control:
pinning, restraining, or throwing enemies. These Maneuvers
allow a combatant to dominate a single opponent by
controlling their movement and actions.
Utility
Utility Maneuvers offer battlefield versatility beyond direct
combat. They may include repositioning, movement tricks,
environmental interaction, intimidation, or setting up
allies for success. These Maneuvers shift the flow of battle
through clever use of space and timing.

Spellcaster
Chapter
Spellcasters in DC20 wield arcane, divine, or primal
forces that reshape reality through intellect, willpower, or
devotion. Their talents go far beyond simple incantations.
Each spell cast is a calculated use of magical power, refined
through discipline and mastery. This system of structured
magic is expressed through:
- Spells: Magical effects that deal damage, heal wounds,
alter terrain, or influence minds.
- Spell Enhancements: Modifiers that increase a spell’s
range, area, duration, or potency.

#### Spells Known

Spellcaster Classes (Bard, Cleric, Druid, Sorcerer,
Spellblade*, Warlock, and Wizard) know a number of
Spells as shown in the Spells Known column of their Class
Tables, plus again Spells learned from Class Features,
Talents, or Spellcaster Path progression (see “Character
Paths” on page 192 for more information).
Spell List
Spellcaster Classes each have different Spell Lists which
specify the types of Spells they’re able to learn. Martial
Classes can gain access to a Spell List through gaining
Spellcaster Path progression or the Spellcasting Expansion
Talent (see “Talents” on page 186 for more information).
Example: The Wizard Class specifies the Arcane as the Spells
they can learn. When the Wizard learns Spells at 1st level or
higher from their Class, they must choose from the Spells
with Arcane listed as a Source (see Spell Sources later in
this chapter). If they gain another Spell Source through the
Spellcaster Expansion Talent, then they can learn new Spells
from any of their Spell Sources.

#### Spell Resources

Each Spell has a minimum number of Action Points (AP)
that must be spent to cast it. Many Spells also include a
minimum number of Mana Points (MP) that must also
be spent to cast them. This is known as the Base Mana
Cost of a Spell.

#### Action Points

Casting any Spell requires spending Action Points (AP).
Spells that affect a single target typically cost 1 AP, while
those that affect multiple targets usually cost 2 AP.

### Mana Points

Some Spells have an MP cost that you must spend to cast
them. The maximum amount of MP you can spend on
a single Spell must be equal to or lower than your Mana
Spend Limit (see “Combat Resources” on page 41 for
more information on Mana Points).
DC Tip: Remember, your Mana Spend Limit is equal to your
Combat Mastery.

#### Spell Characteristics

Components
There are 3 types of Components that a Spell could have:
Verbal, Somatic, and Material. All Spells require Verbal and
Somatic components, unless stated otherwise. A few rare
Spells require Material components. In order to cast any
Spell you must satisfy its Components outlined below.
Verbal: These are the magical words and incantations
needed to manifest the magical effects of a spell. This
represents the PC uttering a few words to cast the Spell.
- Creatures that can hear you will notice that you’re casting
a Spell.
- If you’re silenced by either magical or mundane means
(Muffle Spell or your mouth is gagged) you can’t cast
Spells with a Verbal Component.
- You can still speak underwater to cast a Spell, but you lose
your Breath (see Breath).
DC Tip: From a roleplay perspective, the PC could say a
specific word, a phrase, or a strange sound. It’s just important
for both GM and Player to understand that the PC is making a
sound of some kind when casting a Spell.
Somatic: These are the gestures, movements, and physical
actions needed to control the magical energies within a
Spell.
- Creatures that can see you will notice that you’re casting
a Spell.
- You need to be able to move at least one hand that’s not
holding an object, or have a Spell Focus to satisfy the
Somatic Component.
DC Tip: From a roleplay perspective, the PC could move their
arms around in a grand display or use simple hand gestures.
It’s just important for both GM and Player to understand
that the PC is visibly moving their hand around when
casting a Spell.
Material: These are objects, items, ingredients, and other
physical items needed to cast the Spell. Most Spells do not
have Material Components required to cast them, but those
that do usually either have a high gold cost or use an item
that the GM can determine the rarity of.
- You can’t cast these Spells without having the Material
Component.
- If the Spell consumes the Material Component, you must
replace it before you can cast it again.
- Could have a high gold cost or be rare to obtain the item.
DC Tip: From a roleplay perspective, the PC could weave
the Spell Components into their description of them casting
a Spell. Players that want to add flavor to their spellcasting
can create their own Material Components for a Spell
(like slime from a frog to cast the Oil Slick Spell). But these
“flavor” Components should not be held against them by
the GM, unless the Player wants to track how many Spell
Components they have.

Duration
Spells can either be instantaneous or continuous in their
duration, and some Spells require you to Sustain them in
order to keep them going (see the “Sustained Action” on
page 44 for more information).
- Instantaneous: Instantaneous Spells take effect the
moment they’re performed.
- Continuous: These Spells last a specified amount of
time. They often end early when the affected creature
Succeeds on a subsequent Save against the effect, it or
another creature within 1 Space performs an Action to
end the effect, or some other method stated to end the
effect occurs (such as breaking line of sight).
- Sustained: Some effects require you to Sustain them or
they end.
Range
This is the distance within which a target of the Spell, or
the origin of its Area of Effect, must be. When selecting the
targets of your Spell, they must be within the listed Range
and fulfill any other listed requirement of the Spell (such as
being visible or capable of hearing you). If the chosen Spell
uses an Area of Effect to determine its targets, the origin
of the Spell must be within its listed Range (see “Areas of
Effect” on page 157 for more information).

#### Casting a Spell

In order to cast a Spell, you must know the Spell and spend
the required amount of Resources (Action Points and Mana
Points). When you cast a Spell, you must make a Check
(such as an Attack Check or Spell Check) to determine the
Spell’s degree of success or failure. Your Check is compared
against the target’s Defense (such as PD or AD), the Spell’s
DC, or the target’s Contested Check.

### Enhancements

Enhancements amplify the impact of a character’s offense,
turning well-executed strikes into devastating blows. They
can increase damage, extend range, affect wider areas, or
inflict conditions, allowing spellcaster characters to adapt
to the shifting demands of battle.

#### Spell Enhancements

Spell Enhancements allow you to spend additional AP
or MP to increase the Spell’s potency (such as damage,
duration, range, number of targets, or Area of Effect). Spell
Enhancements are specific to each Spell and are included in
a Spell’s description.
Performing a Spell
Enhancement
When you perform a Spell, you can spend additional MP to
add a Spell Enhancement with the Spell. If the Spell requires
a Check (such as an Attack Check or Spell Check), you
must choose to add the Spell Enhancement before you make
the Check. You can perform the same Spell Enhancement
multiple times per Spell, unless stated otherwise.
Mana Points on AP Enhancements: You can spend 1 MP to
add 2 AP worth of AP Enhancements to a Spell. You can
do this multiple times per Spell.
Example: A Spell has an AP Enhancement called Damage
that allows the caster to spend 1 AP to increase the Spell’s
damage by 1. Alternatively, the caster could spend 1 MP to
add 2 AP worth of AP Enhancements to the Spell, such as
choosing the Damage option twice (total of +2 damage).

#### Enhancement Damage

If an Enhancement deals damage, it deals the same type of
damage as the Spell you’re using to perform the Attack or
Spell, unless stated otherwise.

Categorizing

### Spells

In DC20, every Spell is organized by 3 distinct categories:
its Source, School, and Tags. Together,  these categories
explain where a Spell’s power originates, how it’s
understood in the world, and how it functions within the
rules. This makes every spell in DC20 a fully defined part of
the magical system, rooted in lore, tied to character identity,
and shaped for strategic gameplay.
Spellcasters may gain the ability to draw power from a
Source of Magic through:
- Rigorous study and arcane practice, unlocking secrets
from tomes and teachings (Wizards).
- A deep faith and devotion to a divine being (Clerics).
- Pacts with powerful entities that grant magical gifts in
exchange for service (Warlocks).
- Their bond with the natural world and its primal forces
(Druids).
- Blood inheritance or life-altering origins (Sorcerers).
- Artistic expression and emotional resonance (Bards).
- Sacred oaths, powerful relics, supernatural forces, or
deeply personal sources (Spellblades).

#### Sources of Magic

Sources of Magic represent the origin of magic. All Spells
draw power from at least one of the three fundamental
Sources: Arcane, Divine, or Primal. These Sources
determine how a Spellcaster accesses and channels magic.
Some Spells list 2 or more types as their Source (such as
both Arcane and Primal for many fire Spells).
Arcane
Arcane magic is the invisible energy that permeates through
all of existence, surrounding and connecting every part
of the universe. It is a pure, formless energy that can be
shaped through study, focus, and discipline. Many mortals
learn to harness this power by mastering incantations,
runes, and rituals that channel raw magic into structured,
purposeful spells.
Divine
Divine magic draws power from divine beings (good, evil,
and indifferent), such as deities (greater and lesser gods),
their followers (angels, archons, etc.). or the manifestations
of abstract universal concepts (order, love, peace, war, etc.).
Divine magic is granted to mortals either through faith,
duty, or desperation. Creatures that wield Divine magic are
often servants of their deity, seeking to fulfill their agendas
and grow their following.
Primal
Primal magic draws power from the physical forces of the
universe (land, air, water, fire, plants, animals, and even
the planes themselves). This magic comes from a deep
rooted connection to the living essence of the world and its
elemental energies. Those who wield Primal magic channel
the wild rhythms of existence itself; storms, beasts, natural
disasters, and the oceans.

#### Schools of Magic

Schools of Magic define the lore and philosophy behind
different magical effects. A School of Magic serves as
a way to classify magical effects by answering two key
questions: what kind of action is the spell performing (such
as creating, summoning, altering, or destroying) and the
subject it’s being performed on (such as the elements, time,
or a creature’s body, mind, or spirit). This structure helps
mages, priests, and sages study, understand, and organize
the many forms of magic that shape the world.

#### Astromancy

Astromancy is the magic of space, time, and gravity. Spells
that alter gravity (reversing, decreasing, or increasing
gravity), teleport objects or creatures (directly or through
portals), or alter the flow of time (reversing, slowing,
speeding, or stopping) fall into the School of Astromancy.
Common Spell Tags: Gravity, Motion, Teleportation, Time,
Planes.

#### Conjuration

Conjuration is the magic of summoning things from one
place to another or fabricating something from nothing.
Spells that summon creatures, create objects, or illusions in
the environment fall into the School of Conjuration.
Common Spell Tags: Earth, Illusion, Light, Scent, Sound,
Summoning, Weapon.

#### Divination

Divination is the magic of revealing the unknown and
the unseen, such as hidden knowledge (past, present, or
future) and special vision (the invisible, the illusory, or
distant locations). Spells that grant magical sight, bestow
knowledge, or discern the truth fall into the School
of Divination.

#### Elemental

Elemental is the magic of the natural elements, such as
earth, wind, fire, water, lightning, and corrosion. Spells that
produce effects that manipulate the elementals fall into the
School of Elemental.
Common Spell Tags: Air, Corrosion, Earth, Fire, Lightning,
Water.

#### Enchantment

Enchantment is the magic of influencing other creatures’
minds, such as provoking strong emotions and mental
domination. Spells that create illusions in a creature’s mind
or charm other creatures through fabricated terror, loyalty,
or love fall into the School of Enchantment.
Common Spell Tags: Embolden, Emotions, Illusion, Psychic,
Sense, Thoughts.

#### Invocation

Invocation is the magic of positive energy, such as light, life,
healing, and resurrection. Spells that create light, mend
wounds, cure sicknesses, create spirits, or resurrect the dead
fall into the School of Invocation.
Common Spell Tags: Cleansing, Healing, Light, Radiant,
Resurrection, Spirit.

#### Nullification

Nullification is the magic of negative energy, such as antimagic, anti-life, and darkness. Spells that destroy magic,
spirits, and light fall into the School of Nullification.
Common Spell Tags: Anti-Magic, Shadow, Spirit, Umbral.

#### Transmutation

Transmutation is the magic of transforming one thing
physically into another. Spells that turn creatures into other
creatures (or objects into other objects or creatures) fall into
the School of Transmutation.
Common Spell Tags: Earth, Embolden, Metamorphosis,
Motion, Sense, Weapon.

#### Spell Tags

Spell Tags describe the characteristics of a spell. Some
Tags represent clear in-world effects (such as manipulating
fire), while others exist mainly to support game mechanics
(such as a type of creature the Spell can target). Spell Tags
define how a Spell interacts with the game system, such
as the damage it deals, the Conditions it inflicts, or the
creatures it affects. Certain Ancestry Traits, Class Features,
and Talents reference specific Spell Tags, allowing
characters to specialize in particular types of magic (such as
Fire, Illusion, or Summoning). Spell Tags help players and
GMs quickly understand a Spell’s function, theme, and
how it interacts with other parts of the game.

#### Spell Duel

Prerequisite: Spellcasting Path
During a Spell Duel, the creature casting the Spell is referred
to as the Caster and the opposing creature is referred to as
the Challenger.
If multiple creatures attempt to initiate a Spell Duel, one of
them must be chosen as the Challenger, while the others

can also choose to assist the Caster when a Spell Duel is

Trigger: You see an MP Effect being cast, provided the
Caster or a target is within 5 Spaces of you.
Reaction: You can spend an amount of AP and 1 or more
MP (up to your Mana Spend Limit) to attempt to stop the
Caster’s MP Effect. The amount of AP spent is equal to
the base AP cost of the MP Effect you’re trying to stop.
Contest
Make a Spell Check contested by the Spell Check or Spell
Attack that the Caster makes to cast their MP Effect. If
the Caster’s MP Effect doesn’t require a Check, they have
to make a Spell Check as a Reaction as part of the Spell
Duel. For the purposes of the Spell Duel, the Caster and
Challenger each gain a bonus to their Check equal to
twice the MP spent they spent respectively.
Beta Note: Since enemies don’t have MP costs for their MP
Effects, you can assume their MP Effects cost an amount of
MP equal to half their Level (rounded up).
Outcomes
Success: The Caster’s MP Effect fails and has no effect.
Failure: The Caster’s MP Effect succeeds and takes effect.
The Check bonus gained from MP spent doesn’t apply to
the MP Effect’s Success and Failure Statements.
Tie: The Caster’s MP Effect fails, has no effect, and you
each roll on the Wild Magic Surge Table. The effect from
the table lasts until 1 Round has passed.
Resources Lost: Whatever the result, each creature still
spends all AP, MP, or other resources as normal.
Example: A Caster makes an MP based Spell Attack and a
Challenger attempts to stop it. The Caster’s Spell Attack cost
1 AP and 2 MP. The Challenger must spend 1 AP and at least
1 MP to attempt to stop the Spell Attack. The Caster makes
an Attack Check (since its a Spell Attack) and the Challenger
makes a Spell Check. The Caster gets a +4 bonus to their
Attack Check (2 MP spent) and the Challenger gets a +2
bonus to their Spell Check (1 MP spent). Should the Caster
win the Contest, their MP Effect takes effect but they don’t
include the +4 bonus from MP spent to determine if they
score a Heavy Hit or higher.

#### Combo Spellcasting

Prerequisite: Spellcasting Path
During a Combo Casting, the creature casting the MP
Effect is referred to as the Caster while the other creatures
assisting are called Participants.
Trigger: You can see an MP Effect you know being cast or a
Spell Duel being initiated, provided the MP Effect’s Caster
or at least 1 of its target are within 5 Spaces of you.
Reaction: You can spend an amount of AP equal to the base
AP cost of the MP Effect you are trying to assist (or stop
in the case of a Spell Duel). When you do, the MP Effect
gains a +2 bonus to its Check and Save DC for this turn. If
you’re participating in a Spell Duel, the Challenger you
are helping gains this bonus to their Check to determine the
winner of the Contest.
Spending MP: You can choose to spend 1 or more MP to
add MP Enhancements to the Caster’s MP Effect. The
total MP spent on the MP Effect can’t exceed the Caster’s
Mana Spend Limit.
Example: Casters A & B each have a Mana Spend Limit of
3. Caster A spends 1 AP and 1 MP to cast the Fire Bolt Spell.
Caster B can spend 1 AP and 2 MP as a Reaction to add 2
MP worth of MP Enhancements to Caster A’s Spell. Caster
A then gains a +2 bonus to their Spell Attack and Save DC
for that Spell.
