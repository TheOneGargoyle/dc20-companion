This month’s DC20 Magazine is all about the game’s Challenge Rules! These rules will come in
handy any time you’re looking to present challenges to your players, but don’t want to zoom all
the way in with a full Combat.
We’ve designed these rules to be flexible and let each player at the table maximize their creativity.
Especially when new players aren’t confident in roleplaying yet, these Challenge Rules give
them a place to stretch those creative muscles and gain confidence in those improvisational skills.
They also provide just enough of a framework to provide satisfying results that make rolling dice
still matter during what might otherwise be a purely narritive moment from the DM.
However you choose to use these rules, we’re sure they’ll help you and your players stay creative
and think outside that box!

What is a
Challenge?
A Challenge is a narrative framework in which a complicated
or dynamic obstacle is presented to an adventuring party;
each member will be able to help toward the shared
outcome of the team with a single Check. Players will take
turns describing how their PC will attempt to contribute
toward facing problems and obstacles, and then they’ll
make a type of Check that best matches their description.
A success on an individual Check gets the group closer
to an overall success in the Challenge, while failure on an
individual Check might come with a penalty and won’t
help the group succeed.
Rules &
Requirements
Running a Challenge includes 4 key steps and processes:
1.	 GM sets parameters for the Challenges (DC and Points
needed).
2.	 Players make their Checks.
3.	 Success Points are given out.
4.	 Challenge’s result is adjudicated.

#### Step 1: GM Sets Parameters

Challenges have 2 important parameters:
- Challenge DC
- Success Point Threshold
Challenge DC
Challenges have a singular DC, against which each
Check is made. It represents the difficulty of overcoming
the obstacles presented. It can be calculated with the
formulas below.
Challenge DCs
Easy: Player Save DC–2
Medium: Player Save DC–1
Hard: Player Save DC
Success Point Threshold
This threshold represents the number of Success Points
needed to succeed on the Challenge. It’s defined by the
number of PCs and NPCs contributing to it, but will almost
always be equal to this number. GMs can modulate this
threshold up or down as they deem necessary, but this is
best done when each success represents a tangible effect
on the narrative (collecting pieces of an artifact, activating
switches in a puzzle, etc.).
DC Tip: Adjusting this threshold is often more punishing
and is a worse way to increase or decrease the difficulty of
a Challenge. I suggest modulating the DC up or down before
moving away from the Standard Threshold until you’ve run
many Challenges and have developed a feel for their difficulty.
Success Point Threshold
Easier: Number of PCs–1
Standard: Number of PCs
Harder: Number of PCs + 1

#### Step 2: PCs Make Checks

Each Player describes what their PC is attempting to
contribute toward the Challenge. The GM will then assign
a Check to that description or list multiple options for the
PC to choose from. Players can request alternative Checks
if what the GM called for didn’t match with what they
had in mind. This process is designed to be collaborative
between GM and PCs.
DC Tip: The GM can propose a list of Checks and actions
that can be taken in the Challenge. These should be seen as
suggestions and options to help guide the players in making a
choice, so they shouldn’t restrict choices.
Example: A locked door could be bashed down (Athletics
Check), picked open (Trickery Check), or blown apart
(Spell Check).

A bothersome guard could be deftly avoided (Stealth Check),
paid off (Influence Check), or scared away (Intimidate Check).

A wild animal could be wrestled into submission (Athletics
Check), calmed down (Animal Check), or given what it wants
(Nature Check).

#### Step 3: Success Points Earned
 Earned

Each Player’s Check result determines the number of
Success Points they earn for their party as shown below.
- Success: PC earns 1 Success Point, and 1 additional
Success Point if it’s a Critical Success.
- Success (each 5): PC earns 1 additional Success Point.
- Failure: The player earns no Success Points, and
potentially a penalty.
- Critical Failure: The player causes the party to lose 1
Success Point.
DC Tip: GMs may choose to ignore the penalty for Critical
Failure in a challenge, especially Easy Challenges, since this
can be very punishing and almost guarantee the group fails.

#### Step 4: Adjudicating The Results
 Results

Once Checks are made, use the following guidance to
evaluate the results.
Individual Success or Failure
There might be additional bonuses that come with
succeeding or failing on a Check. Success might grant a
PC additional knowledge or an item, while failure might
cause you to take damage or suffer another penalty.
These rewards and penalties happen individually for each
Check a PC makes.
DC Tip: It’s totally fine to NOT do anything for these and
just focus on the group Success or Failure. I usually don’t do
anything for an individual success and focus on the rewards
for a GROUP success. Then for failures, I usually only give out
penalties for individual failures and may or may not have a
GROUP failure penalty.
Group Success or Failure
Challenges require a certain number of Success Points
in order for a group to succeed. The number of Success
Points earned by the end of the Challenge determines the
outcome. Challenges often have an overall penalty for a
group failure, but aren’t required. Sometimes missing out
on the rewards is enough. Sometimes a Group Success
isn’t needed (usually in a Combat Challenge) because the
reward could be seen as not taking any damage.
DC Tip: Make sure that even failed Challenges move the
course of the game forward. You don’t want your PCs to be
stuck after failing a Challenge.
Rewards and Penalties
Challenges often give out rewards and penalties, sometimes
to individuals and almost always to the group based on
the overall success of their efforts. Below is a list of typical
rewards and penalties designed to inspire your own.
Rewards
- Inspiration
- Information
- Temp HP
- Consumable Items
- Magic Items
- Semi-permanent Boons
Penalties
- Damage
- Condition
- Loss of time
- Loss of Rest Points
- Level(s) of Exhaustion
- Story or plot setback
- Trigger a Group Save
against some kind of effect
- Ramping Penalty Concept
(gets worse and worse the
more of them there are)
- Semi-permanent Bane

#### Additional Rules

Resources
PCs don’t spend any SP, MP, or any other resources during
Challenges. You can use Spells, Maneuvers, Techniques,
Class Features, and Ancestry Traits as flavor to better
describe how your PC is interacting with the Challenge,
but you don’t gain the effects of that ability.
Example: If you know your allies need help in a time of
need and you wanted to cast a Bless Spell, that’d simply
be the description of how you’re helping in this Challenge.
You’d make a Spell Check, but your party wouldn’t gain the
benefits of the Bless Spell for the Challenge (that would be
overpowered).
Repeated Checks
If a PC repeats the same narrative action and uses the same
Check as another PC, that Check is made with DisADV.
This is to prevent Players from saying Oh yeah, me too!
This ensures that each action taken during a Challenge has
a purpose and enough room to breathe on its own within the
story that the Challenge is trying to tell.
Helping Allies
PCs might want to help an ally instead of performing their
own specific action. This is totally fine, but the PC must
still make a Check that relates to how they’re helping their
ally. They can’t simply grant them a Help Die or anything
along those lines.
DC Tip: Players can’t take the Help Action during Challenges,
but they can narratively describe helping then make a Check
that best matches HOW they help.

ADV & DisADV on Checks
PCs who have permanent or situational ADV or DisADV
on a certain Check due to Features, Spells, or Equipment
make that Check with ADV or DisADV as they would
normally. If the Challenge presents a unique situation
where the GM feels that this ADV or DisADV wouldn’t
apply, they can rule that the Player ignore it for that Check.
Challenge
Guidance
Now that you know how Challenges work, here’s a more
in-depth guide to give you a better understanding and
some examples.

#### Challenge Outline

A Challenge follows this general format, but can be tailored
to any situations as needed by the GM.
1.	 GM presents the party with a complicated or dynamic
obstacle.
2.	 Players discuss with each other how their PC would
respond and what they plan to do.
3.	 Once a Player has made a decision, they describe what
their PC wants to do.
4.	 GM assigns a type of Check based on the description
given (or provides the Player with options to choose
from, which can be done in collaboration between the
Player and GM). Player doesn’t roll until the GM calls
for their Check.
5.	 Once steps 3 and 4 have been completed for each
PC, the GM asks each Player to make their Check
in whatever order the GM chooses (one at a time, in
groups, all at once, etc.).
6.	 Chosen player rolls their Check and declares the result.
7.	 GM declares if the Check succeeds or fails, and how
many Success Points they earn, if any.
8.	 Any rewards or penalties earned by a Check can be
awarded by the GM immediately after the roll or at the
end of the Challenge (GM’s choice).
9.	 Repeat steps 6, 7, and 8 until each Player has acted.
10.	GM reveals if the group succeeded or failed, along with
any additional rewards or penalties earned.
11.	The GM narratively describes what happened during
the Challenge, allowing the Players to describe as
much of the outcome as the GM prefers.

#### Why Use Challenges?

All 3 Pillars: Challenges can be used across all 3 Pillars
of the game (Social, Combat, Exploration) and are
outlined below.
Save Time: Challenges are a fun and open-ended way
to resolve dramatic moments that take less time and
mechanical effort to resolve than something like a combat
encounter, elaborate heist, or murder mystery puzzle.
They’re incredibly useful when moving from one story beat
to the next as they provide a fast and structured way to do so.
Unlock Creativity: Challenges still allow players to express
the strengths and personality of their characters, opening
up the narrative for them to use their abilities and creative
thinking in new, unique ways.
Epic Story Telling: Challenges are meant to provide the
opportunity for players to take the lead on the narrative,
with each player describing their actions in the Challenge
and working with the other players to describe their
efforts. The dice then help add an element of chance and
uncertainty, helping tell the story alongside the players.
Exciting Scenes: Challenges represent a way to craft
cinematic scenes. Action sequences like chases, jailbreaks,
or valiant defenses can drag when using free play or turnby-turn combat, so Challenges offer a way to incorporate
those moments in an exciting, fast manner.
Fail Forward: On a Group Success or even a Group Failure,
the end result should progress the story forward. If the party
fails, they might suffer penalties, but this failure shouldn’t
completely stop the momentum of the story.
Example: If the PCs are trying to do an Exploration
Challenge to open a door or circumvent an obstacle in their
way, the result of the Challenge should still be that they get
through that door. Failure adds an additional cost. Did they
lose time? Did the enemies learn of their presence? You CAN
have the failure penalty be that they can’t enter, but make
sure this doesn’t lead to a narrative dead end. Another way
forward should be available, perhaps a more dangerous,
less ideal one.

Types of
Challenges
Challenges exist in all 3 pillars of play: Combat, Exploration,
and Social. They all use the same ruleset, but tend to resolve
themselves slightly differently. The success and failure
conditions, as well as the narrative resolutions, you’ll find
in each pillar are catered to the type of gameplay you find
within them. Below are a set of examples and tendencies on
these variations and will hopefully help you see how you
could use them in your games.

#### Combat Challenges

Combat Challenges are meant to represent the party’s
efforts to handle enemies in a combat scenario. Typically
the enemies are weaker than the party, and could be easily
defeated. Combat Challenges shouldn’t include encounters
that may pose a serious threat to the party.
DC Tip: At the end of a Combat Challenge, the PCs kill, or
otherwise incapacitate, the enemies. The purpose of the
Challenge isn’t to see if they CAN defeat the combatants,
it’s to tell a story of HOW it happened in a quick and
cinematic way.
Example Uses: Fighting a group of wolves that ambushed
the party, trying to capture a single large beast, or guarding
a location from waves of bandits.
Usual Rewards
- Combat Inspiration
- Combat boons
- Information on the enemy creatures’ Combat Mechanics
- Features for when they have a Combat in the future
against the combatants
- Not taking damage
Usual Penalties
- Taking Damage
- Death of an NPC
- Damage to an object or structure
Example Combat Challenge: The party must run through
a burning village with a hoard of zombies that blocks their
way to try and save an NPC in danger.
- Individual Success: Success Points only
- Individual Failure: 1 True damage
- Group Success: They save the NPC’s life
- Group Failure: Each PC who failed takes 1 additional
damage
- Group Failure by more than 1: You suffer the effects of a
Group Failure, and the NPC dies

#### Exploration Challenges

Exploration Challenges provide a way to represent the party
coming into conflict with the environment or themselves
rather than specific enemies. These can be used for activities
such as working together to scale a steep mountainside,
trying to identify and set up a camp site, navigating through
a tangled forest, or enduring hazardous weather conditions.
DC Tip: These challenges can still have a combat or a social
aspect to it with which the PCs can engage. The key to all
Challenges is to give the Players enough things to interact
with so they can come up with new ways to use their PCs.
Example Uses: Setting up a campsite in a dangerous area,
a trap room, harvesting or skinning a monster, traversing
dangerous terrain.
Usual Rewards
- Finding extra treasure
- Learning secret information
- Arriving early to a destination
Usual Penalties
- Losing time
- Losing Rest Points
- Taking damage from environmental hazards
- Gaining a level of Exhaustion
- Being discovered or seen (if PCs were attempting
subterfuge)
Example Exploration Challenge: The party must sneak
through a cave that is full of sleeping creatures. The floor
is slippery, but there are some hand holds on the walls
for climbing.
- Individual Success: Success Points only
- Individual Failure: No penalty
- Group Success: You get across the cave undetected
- Group Failure: The creatures all wake up and attack you,
starting a Deadly Combat

#### Social Challenge

Social Challenges are used when the party attempts
to engage in an important or delicate social situation,
especially when the whole party is involved. These have
a stronger focus on verbal and diplomatic interactions with
others, but might also involve performance, deception,
displays of prowess and confidence, and many other forms
of engaging with other intelligent creatures.
DC Tip: The rewards for Social Challenges can change wildly
based on the context of what’s going on. Convincing a king to
use their forces in your favor could be a HUGE reward in the
big picture of a campaign.
Example Uses: Earning the trust of a king, getting into
a guild, influencing a court trial proceeding, or calming a
group of citizens after a dragon attack.
Usual Rewards
- Unlocking a new ally or resource
- Inspiration
- Additional treasure or magic items
Usual Penalties
- Not being able to do something as easily or at all
- Loss of gold
- Not getting everything the PCs wanted
Example Combat Challenge: You need to talk to a quest
giver NPC and convince or impress them enough to gain
a specific quest.
- Individual Success: Increase of reputation and respect
with the NPCs involved
- Individual Failure: Loss of reputation and respect with
the NPCs involved
- Group Success: Gain the contract and a number of
Healing Potions equal to the number of PCs
- Group Failure: Gain the contract, but gain no additional
rewards (or a lesser reward if the GM is feeling generous)
